## Lessons

1. The morning decision file ran two days in a row for the first time. Progress is real, but
   incomplete: the July 6 decision file has no journal pair. A morning decision file and an
   end-of-day journal are a matched set — one tells the story of intent, the other tells the
   story of outcome. Running only half the pair on July 6 means we cannot reconstruct what
   actually happened that day from the record alone. Both halves must run or neither is useful.

2. Writing explicit buy-price targets in the decision file (e.g., MSFT at or below $310 in
   cautious posture, at or below $333 in normal posture) converts patience from a passive state
   into a conditional waiting strategy. When a price level is committed to in writing, the
   morning review becomes a mechanical gate check rather than a fresh judgment call under
   market conditions. Pre-committing to numbers is how discipline survives a fast-moving tape.

3. SPY's total return since our June 16 inception has moved from -0.76% (July 3) to -0.37%
   today. The benchmark is recovering. The three-week story so far is: SPY fell, we held cash,
   SPY partially came back. That sequence has produced a momentary "outperformance" of 0.37 pp
   — but it is not credit to our research or timing. The real test begins when SPY crosses its
   June 16 level. The account must deploy into names it understands at prices that make sense,
   or the cash will simply underperform a rising benchmark indefinitely.
