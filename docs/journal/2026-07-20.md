## Lessons

1. **Cash protection during a down week is passive, not earned.** SPY fell roughly 1.30
   percentage points between July 14 and today. Our gap versus SPY widened to +1.12 pp as a
   result. This looks good in the table but means nothing for the thesis: we held cash because
   nothing cleared our buy threshold on July 13, not because we predicted a fall. When SPY
   recovers, the gap will close again at no benefit to us. The measure that matters is not
   whether we avoided this week's decline but whether we will be positioned to compound at a
   better-than-SPY rate over years.

2. **A 1.30-pp SPY decline over four days raises a concrete question we cannot answer.** The
   last decision file (July 13) put MSFT at a -2.15% margin of safety against a $378 base and
   V at -15.6% against a $297 base. A market-wide decline of that magnitude could move either
   name meaningfully. Without a morning review record for July 14–18, we do not know whether
   MSFT or V crossed into buy territory on any of those days. That gap is permanent — we cannot
   reconstruct prices that were never examined in writing. This is the direct cost of the
   missing process.

3. **Six trading days since the last decision file is too long to leave the posture unreviewed.**
   The July 13 decision was correct for July 13. It said FOMC meets July 28–29. That meeting
   is now eight calendar days away. The macro calendar that drove the cautious posture is now
   close enough to require a fresh written review, not a default carry-forward. Tomorrow's
   morning cycle must produce a new decision file that re-prices all three thesis names against
   current quotes, re-states the margin-of-safety bar, and explicitly decides whether the
   cautious posture still applies.
