## Lessons

1. A recurring failure that is recorded but not fixed structurally is not a lesson learned —
   it is a documented habit. The missing morning decision file has been called out in three
   previous journals (2026-06-18, 2026-06-19, 2026-06-22) and has been written into the
   PLAYBOOK. It kept happening anyway. The same is now true of the end-of-day journal. The
   only remedy that works is a structural one: the morning and end-of-day phases need to be
   enforced or triggered automatically, not left to discretion.

2. Eight trading days passed without a journal. During that time, SPY moved (the total return
   changed from -0.84% on 2026-06-22 to -0.76% today, meaning SPY recovered roughly 0.08
   percentage points). We have no written record of what any of those days looked like, whether
   any names came close to our buy threshold, or whether any process event occurred. The journal
   is the only way to make the account's inaction legible. Without it, we are not managing
   money — we are just watching a number sit at $1,000,000.

3. The July 4 holiday creates a natural pause. When markets reopen on Monday 2026-07-07, the
   morning cycle must produce a decision file. If the account has been holding $1,000,000 in
   cash for over three weeks without a single order, the Monday morning decision file needs to
   answer honestly: did every name on the watchlist fail the margin-of-safety test on every one
   of those days, or did the process simply not run? One answer is disciplined patience; the
   other is passive inaction. Only a written record distinguishes them.
