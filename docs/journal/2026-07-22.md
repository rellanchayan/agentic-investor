## Lessons

1. **The cautious posture adopted on July 13 had named expiry conditions; they have passed
   unreviewed.** The July 13 decision was explicit: cautious posture was set because of a
   specific geopolitical event, an upcoming macro calendar (CPI, Warsh testimony, PPI,
   FOMC July 28-29), and a valuation context (~89th-percentile forward P/E). The first three
   items on that list were scheduled events in the week of July 14 — now nine days ago. A
   posture set for specific conditions should be reaffirmed with current data once those
   conditions have passed, or it has effectively expired. Tomorrow's morning review should
   not treat "cautious" as the standing baseline; it should re-derive the posture from scratch
   using current prices, the current macro picture, and current fair-value estimates.

2. **SPY's -0.41% move tells us nothing about whether our specific candidates are cheaper.**
   The 9-basis-point decline in SPY between Tuesday and today is a broad-market observation.
   MSFT, V, and AAPL each have their own price movements, driven partly by earnings, macro,
   and sector rotation that may diverge sharply from SPY. The fact that SPY fell does not
   mean MSFT is now at $333 or below. Only a morning price check with live quotes answers
   that. We cannot shortcut "is anything cheap enough?" by looking at index returns.

3. **Seven trading days of identical EOD journals — $1,000,000, no trades, no fills — signals
   that the morning cycle is not running, not that every day was reviewed and found wanting.**
   If the morning cycle were running and consistently concluding "hold," the journals would
   show different prices, updated thesis assessments, and a reaffirmed posture. The sameness
   of the last seven EOD records is evidence that the process is not executing, not that it
   is executing and reaching the same disciplined conclusion each day. These two states are
   not the same and should not be described the same way.
