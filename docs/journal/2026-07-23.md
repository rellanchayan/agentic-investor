## Lessons

1. **A 1.67 pp lead over SPY is now meaningless without a single share purchased.** SPY has
   fallen 1.67% since our account opened. Because we hold cash, we are 1.67 pp "ahead" on
   paper. But holding cash in a falling market is not the same as beating the market: when SPY
   recovers, the lead evaporates without our having taken any risk or made any decision. The
   benchmark for this portfolio is beating SPY over years by owning better businesses at better
   prices — not by timing the market from a cash position. Thirty-seven calendar days in, the
   account has no holdings and no realized experience of any kind.

2. **The morning decision file was written today, and that matters.** After a run of missing
   morning write-ups flagged in the journals for 2026-07-03, 2026-07-10, 2026-07-14, and
   2026-07-21, today's record is complete: a decision file explains the posture and the
   specific reasons each name was passed on, and this journal closes the day. One complete day
   is the baseline the system should operate at every trading day, not an exception worth
   noting. The standard going forward is: if a morning decision file does not exist, the EOD
   journal must say so explicitly and explain why.

3. **MSFT is the closest candidate and still far from buyable under cautious conditions.** At
   $379.68 against a base fair value of $378.07, MSFT is essentially at fair value — barely
   any discount at all, let alone 18%. The decision file correctly calculated the cautious-
   posture entry threshold at approximately $310. That is a 18% fall from today's price. Given
   that SPY has now fallen 1.67% from our account-open date and valuations remain elevated at
   20.3x forward earnings, there is no obvious near-term catalyst to bring MSFT to $310. The
   right posture remains: watch the price, do not chase, do not lower the bar.
