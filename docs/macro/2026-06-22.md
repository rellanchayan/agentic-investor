# Market Weather — Monday, June 22, 2026

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The US stock market is sitting near historically expensive valuations (Shiller CAPE at the 98th percentile), inflation is running hot at 4.2%, the Fed has a slight hike bias, and a critical inflation print (May PCE) lands Thursday — demand a wider margin of safety before any new buy.

---

## Key terms, briefly

- **SPY** is the fund that tracks the 500 largest US companies. It is our benchmark. "The market" means SPY.
- **200-day average** is the average SPY closing price over the past ~200 trading days. SPY above it = general uptrend. SPY below it = general downtrend.
- **The Fed / interest rates**: the US central bank sets short-term borrowing rates. Higher rates tend to push stock prices down; lower rates do the opposite.
- **Inflation (CPI / PCE)**: how fast prices are rising across the economy. Hot inflation makes the Fed more likely to raise rates.
- **Earnings season**: the few weeks each quarter when companies report profits. Can move individual stocks sharply. We are between seasons right now.

---

## Where SPY stands

SPY closed Friday at **744.82**, which is **8.1% above** its 200-day moving average of 688.84. That signals an ongoing long-term uptrend. However, being well above the 200-day average also means we have recovered a lot from any recent lows — it does not mean stocks are cheap.

## Valuation: expensive by history

The **Shiller CAPE** (a smoothed, inflation-adjusted earnings ratio) sits at roughly **39.9** — a level reached by only 2% of all historical readings. The simpler trailing price-to-earnings ratio is about **25.5x**, slightly above its long-term average. Neither number screams "buy everything." At these levels, a careful investor demands a real discount before putting new money to work.

## The Fed and interest rates: on hold, slight hike bias

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its June 17 meeting — the fourth meeting in a row with no change. This was new chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting. The Fed's internal projections ("dot plot") show a median rate of 3.8% by year-end, meaning a **rate hike is on the table** for later in 2026 if inflation does not cool. Nine of the meeting participants expected at least one hike this year; only one expected a cut.

Higher rates are a headwind for stocks: they make bonds more attractive and raise the discount rate applied to future earnings.

---

## Inflation: still too hot

- **CPI (May 2026):** +4.2% year-over-year — the highest reading since April 2023, driven by a 23.5% annual surge in energy prices. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) came in at +2.9%.
- **PCE (April 2026, most recent):** +3.8% year-over-year; core PCE +3.3%. The Fed's target is 2%.

The one positive piece of news: a US-Iran ceasefire framework was agreed in mid-June, and crude oil has fallen to 3-month lows (WTI ~$81/barrel, Brent ~$84). If sustained, cheaper oil could drag energy inflation lower in coming months. But that effect has not yet shown up in the data.

## Geopolitics: US-Iran deal

A US-Iran ceasefire framework (agreed mid-June) is aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz and dismantling Iran's nuclear program. Oil prices have already dropped 4%+ on the news. Active negotiations continue. If the deal holds, energy prices could fall further, helping inflation. If it collapses, oil could spike again and worsen the inflation picture.

## What this means for us today

We are in **cautious** mode. That means:

1. **We do not lower our standards to find something to buy.** Our fair-value estimates already show AAPL at a 54% premium to base fair value and V at a 11% premium. Neither clears our margin of safety at today's prices. MSFT is essentially at fair value (margin of safety of ~1%).
2. **We hold our full $1,000,000 in cash** until a quality company is priced at a real discount.
3. **We watch Thursday's PCE closely.** A hot number could push rates higher and give us better entry points.
4. **The US-Iran oil development is worth monitoring** — cheaper energy is genuinely positive for inflation and the broader economy if it holds.
5. We do not panic, and we do not do nothing forever. We keep researching new names so we are ready to move when the price is right.

The playbook says: "A missed buy costs nothing; an overpriced buy costs money." Today, that principle rules.
