## What the market is doing right now

**SPY** (the fund that holds the 500 largest US companies — our benchmark) last traded at **$741.97**, sitting about 7.0% above its **200-day average** of $693.56. A price above the 200-day average is a sign the market is in a general long-term uptrend — a mild positive.

But this morning the short-term picture is worse. **S&P 500 futures are down roughly 1%** before the open (Polymarket traders put only a 12% chance the market opens higher). The S&P 500 index already fell 0.45% on Tuesday, closing at 7,503. The drag today comes primarily from two things happening at once: a geopolitical shock from the Middle East, and a significant scheduled Fed event this afternoon.

---

## Geopolitical shock: Iran ceasefire is over

President Trump announced this morning that the US-Iran ceasefire is "over" and fresh US strikes on Iran were reported. That sent **crude oil prices surging more than 5%**, which matters for us in two ways:

1. **Direct economic hit**: Energy costs ripple through almost every business — shipping, manufacturing, consumers' wallets. Higher oil is a headwind to earnings.
2. **Inflation fuel**: Oil was already a major driver of the current 4.2% CPI reading. A 5% one-day oil spike could push the June CPI report (due next Tuesday) even higher. Hotter inflation gives the Federal Reserve more reason to raise interest rates, which hurts stock prices.

Additionally, Middle East conflict raises the risk of disruption to oil shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint through which roughly 20% of global oil passes.

---

## Interest rates and the Fed — the big event today

The **Federal Reserve** controls short-term interest rates. **Higher rates make borrowing more expensive** for companies and consumers, which tends to slow the economy and push stock prices down. Lower rates do the opposite.

Rates are currently held at **3.50–3.75%** — unchanged for four consecutive meetings. But the tone has shifted. The dot plot from the June meeting (the Fed's own forecast of where rates are headed) showed that **9 of 18 officials now expect at least one rate hike by year-end 2026** — a meaningful tightening bias. The median forecast for 2026 inflation (measured by PCE, the Fed's preferred gauge) was revised up to 3.6%, well above the Fed's 2% target.

**Today's key event: FOMC minutes released at 2:00 PM ET.** These are the detailed notes from the June 16-17 meeting. They carry unusual importance because new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh gave no forward guidance at the press conference, leaving the written transcript as the primary clue to the Fed's thinking about rate hikes. Markets will parse every word. If the minutes sound more hawkish than expected, stocks could sell off further this afternoon. This is a live risk event for today's session.

## Inflation

**CPI (Consumer Price Index)** measures how fast everyday prices are rising. The Fed targets 2% per year.

The most recent reading (May 2026): headline CPI rose **4.2% year-over-year**, driven heavily by energy prices. Core CPI (which strips out food and energy) is at 2.82% — above target but more moderate. With oil surging again today, the June CPI reading (released next Tuesday, July 14) could come in even hotter.

## Is the market expensive or cheap right now?

Expensive. Several valuation measures point the same direction:

- **Trailing P/E ratio**: roughly 25–32 times earnings depending on the method used. The long-run historical median is around 16–18 times.
- **Shiller CAPE ratio** (a longer-run smoothed measure): 39.9 as of May 2026 — approximately **125% above its historical average**. This is among the most stretched readings in modern history.
- **Forward P/E**: 20.4 times expected earnings — above both the 5-year average (19.9) and the 10-year average (19.0).

An expensive market does not mean a crash is coming. Prices can stay elevated for a long time, and strong earnings growth can eventually "grow into" a high P/E. But it does mean there is very little room for bad news. Any negative surprise — a hot CPI, a hawkish FOMC minutes release, a disappointing earnings report, an oil shock — hits harder when starting valuations are stretched. All four of those risks are active right now.
