# Market Weather — 2026-06-16

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The market is trading well above its long-run average price,
valuations are elevated by historical standards, inflation is running hot at 4.2%, and
the Fed issues its next rate decision tomorrow — we demand a larger discount before
putting any new money to work.

---

## What the numbers say this morning

**SPY and its 200-day average**
SPY (the fund that holds the 500 largest US companies, and our benchmark) closed yesterday
at $753.06. Its 200-day average — the average price over roughly the last 200 trading days,
a rough measure of the long-run trend — sits at $687.34. SPY is about 9.6% above that
average. Being above the 200-day average is a healthy sign (the market is in an uptrend),
but being nearly 10% above it means the market has run up a fair amount. There is less
of a "discount" available to patient buyers right now.

**Futures this morning**
S&P 500 E-Mini futures were up about +1.2% and Nasdaq 100 E-Mini futures were up about
+2.0% in pre-market trading. At the open, however, markets were largely flat — traders
appear to be waiting to hear from the Federal Reserve tomorrow before making big moves.

**Valuations — is the market cheap or expensive?**
Two standard measures say the market is on the pricey side:
- Trailing P/E ratio (price divided by the past year of earnings): roughly 25-32, depending
  on the source. The long-run historical average is around 15-20.
- Shiller CAPE ratio (a smoothed, inflation-adjusted version that averages 10 years of
  earnings): 40.7, which is about 26% above its own long-run average of ~32.

Neither number screams "crash imminent," but both say that stocks in general are not cheap.
When the market is expensive, patient investors simply require a bigger discount before buying
any individual company.

**Inflation (CPI)**
The most recent inflation reading (May CPI, released June 10) came in at 4.2% year-over-year.
That is meaningfully above the Fed's 2% target. Much of the spike has been driven by energy
prices, which had been elevated by tensions in the Middle East.

**Interest rates and the Fed**
The Federal Reserve (the US central bank) currently has its short-term interest rate set at
3.50%–3.75%. The Fed's policy committee (the FOMC) is meeting right now — Tuesday June 16
through Wednesday June 17. Their decision and updated economic forecasts (the "dot plot")
come out Wednesday afternoon at approximately 2:00 pm ET. This is also the first major
meeting for the new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh.

With inflation still at 4.2%, virtually nobody expects a rate cut tomorrow (futures markets
put the odds of a hold at ~97%). The dot plot (the committee's forecast of where rates will
go) and the new chair's press conference will be closely watched for clues about cuts later
in 2026 or into 2027. Higher rates for longer mean borrowing costs stay elevated, which is
a headwind for stock valuations.

## The geopolitical wildcard: US-Iran deal and oil

Over the weekend and Monday, President Trump announced that the US and Iran had reached an
interim peace agreement, with a formal signing ceremony set for Friday in Switzerland.
This matters because Iran's strait of Hormuz is a chokepoint for global oil tanker traffic.
The prospect of it reopening sent crude oil down roughly 4-5% on Monday. Cheaper oil
reduces one of the main drivers of the current 4.2% inflation reading, which is good news
for the Fed's path back to 2%.

This is a positive development, but it is also not yet final (the deal is signed on Friday),
and geopolitical agreements can fall apart.

## What this means for us today

We are cautious. The market is not in freefall — it is above its 200-day trend line and
futures are positive — but the combination of elevated valuations, hot inflation, and a live
Fed meeting tomorrow means this is not a moment to be eager. Our founding principle says
"a missed buy costs nothing; an overpriced buy costs money." Today we live by that rule.

In practice, cautious means:
- We still read and write research, and update theses.
- If a stock we have been watching appears genuinely undervalued (our thesis work shows a
  meaningful margin of safety even after demanding an extra cushion), we can still use a
  slice of our $5,000 daily budget.
- We do not rush. We do not buy something just because it feels good after a big green day.
- We pay close attention to the Fed announcement Wednesday afternoon. It could reset the
  whole picture.
