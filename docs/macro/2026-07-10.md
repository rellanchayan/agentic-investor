## The big events this week and next

### 1. US-Iran military conflict (active right now)

On Tuesday July 7, the United States struck more than 80 targets inside Iran after Iranian forces attacked US military infrastructure in Bahrain and Kuwait. Iran retaliated again. On Wednesday July 8, Trump declared the ceasefire "over" and strikes resumed. Oil markets responded sharply: Brent crude jumped 5.4% to $78.19/barrel, and WTI crude rose 4.4% to $73.52.

The International Monetary Fund responded by cutting its 2026 global growth forecast from 3.5% to 3.0%, citing the energy shock from this conflict. An active shooting war between the US and a major oil producer is not a normal background condition. Stocks have absorbed it so far, but any escalation — particularly anything involving the Strait of Hormuz, through which a large portion of global oil flows — would be a genuine shock to markets.

Source: [Wikipedia: 2026 Iran War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Iran_war), [TheStreet July 8](https://www.thestreet.com/stock-market-today/stock-market-today-dow-jones-sp-500-nasdaq-updates-july-8-2026), [Bloomberg on oil/Iran](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2026-07-08/us-strikes-iran-can-t-help-gloating-and-oil-prices-go-up)

### 2. CPI inflation report — Tuesday July 14 at 8:30am ET (4 days away)

The Bureau of Labor Statistics releases the June 2026 consumer price index (CPI) on Tuesday morning. CPI measures how fast everyday prices are rising. If inflation comes in hotter than expected, the Federal Reserve has less room to cut interest rates, which generally pushes stock prices lower. If it comes in cool, pressure on rates eases and stocks tend to rise. This report is one of the most watched economic releases in any month.

Source: [BLS CPI release schedule](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/news_release/cpi.htm)

### 3. Major bank earnings — starting Tuesday July 14

Q2 earnings season is just getting started. Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, and Morgan Stanley all report Tuesday July 14. JPMorgan Chase reports Wednesday July 15. Banks are the first major group to report each quarter and their results set the tone for whether the economy and credit conditions are healthy. Big Tech names (Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, Meta, Amazon) are expected later in the month.

Analysts are projecting S&P 500 earnings grew +22% in Q2 2026 — if that holds, it would be the second consecutive quarter of 20%+ earnings growth, which is a real positive. But guidance (what companies say about the future) will matter as much as backward-looking numbers, especially given the Iran conflict and soft jobs data.

Source: [Plus500 Q2 2026 Earnings Preview](https://us.plus500.com/en/newsandmarketinsights/q2-2026-earnings-season), [Earnings Whispers calendar](https://www.earningswhispers.com/calendar)

The Federal Reserve's next interest-rate meeting is in 19 days. The Fed has held its benchmark rate at 3.50%–3.75% for four consecutive meetings. No rate cut or hike is widely expected at this meeting, but the tone of the statement and Fed Chair press conference will matter. If CPI on July 14 comes in hot, expectations for future cuts will fade, which can weigh on stocks.

## Is the market expensive or cheap?

By historical standards, the market looks expensive. The S&P 500's trailing price-to-earnings ratio is about 25x — that puts it in the 85th percentile of all historical readings, meaning stocks have been cheaper than this 85% of the time in history. The Shiller CAPE ratio (which smooths earnings over 10 years) suggests the market may be substantially overvalued versus very long-run norms.

The counter-argument: the forward P/E (based on the next twelve months of expected earnings) is about 20x, which is near the 5-year average and not alarming. If earnings really do grow 22% in Q2, that helps justify current prices.

Our view: the forward P/E optimism is built on earnings estimates that could be cut if oil stays high, the conflict widens, or the soft jobs market hurts consumer spending. We use the more conservative trailing/historical lens. The market is not obviously a screaming bargain.

Source: [Current Market Valuation](https://www.currentmarketvaluation.com/models/price-earnings.php), [InvestSnips P/E guide](https://investsnips.com/sp500-pe-ratio/)

## The jobs market

The June 2026 jobs report (released July 2) was a significant miss: the economy added only 57,000 jobs, against expectations of 113,000 — roughly half of what forecasters expected. The unemployment rate held at 4.2%. Worse, the April and May numbers were revised down by a combined 74,000 jobs. One month of weak data is noise; three months pointing in the same direction is a signal. Wage growth (+3.5% year-over-year) is slowing too. A weakening labor market typically leads to less consumer spending, which hurts corporate earnings.

Source: [Yahoo Finance / BLS jobs](https://finance.yahoo.com/economy/articles/bls-jobs-report-57k-half-161200954.html), [BLS Employment Situation](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm)

## What this means for us today

We hold 100% cash ($1,000,000) with zero positions and a daily buy budget of $5,000. Cautious posture means we should not rush to put that budget to work. Here is the discipline in practical terms:

- We do not buy into imminent uncertainty. CPI in 4 days and major bank earnings next week could move prices materially. Patience costs nothing when we have no positions at risk.
- We demand a bigger discount before buying. In a cautious environment, we want to see a name trading at a meaningful discount to our fair value (base case), not just at or slightly below it. Looking at our candidates: AAPL is 60% above our fair value estimate (do not buy), Visa is 20% above our base fair value (do not buy), and MSFT is trading just 1.2% above its base fair value — barely interesting even in normal conditions, and not compelling in cautious conditions.
- We watch for a better entry, not a worse one. If the CPI print is benign and bank earnings are solid, prices may hold or rise, and we may have missed a small move. That is acceptable — we are long-term investors. If they are bad, we may get better prices next week. The asymmetry favors waiting.
- The Iran conflict remains a genuine tail risk. An escalation into the Strait of Hormuz or a broader regional conflict could move oil to $90+ and hit stocks hard. We do not need to be long before that is resolved.

**Today's posture: CAUTIOUS.** One-sentence reason: active military conflict with oil spike, CPI report in 4 days, bank earnings starting next week, and a market trading at historically expensive trailing valuations all argue for demanding a bigger discount before buying.
