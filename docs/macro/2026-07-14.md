# Market Weather — 2026-07-14

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** June inflation came in well below expectations this morning, which is genuine good news, but the market is still priced at historically expensive levels, the Fed may raise rates at its July 28-29 meeting, Iran tensions persist, and all three of our watchlist names remain above their fair values.

---

## What the numbers say

**SPY vs its 200-day average**
SPY last traded at $751.41. Its 200-day moving average — the average closing price over the last ~200 trading days — is $695.34. That means SPY is about 8% above the long-term average. Being above the 200-day average is a sign the market is in a healthy uptrend, not a crash or a bear market. But it also means the market is not on sale. We are buying at a premium relative to the recent past.

**Futures this morning (before open)**
Futures were mixed heading into the open. S&P 500 futures were down a hair (-0.06%) at 7,558. Nasdaq futures were up 0.47%. Dow futures were down 0.27%. Not a big move in either direction — the market is absorbing the CPI print and waiting on Fed Chair Warsh's testimony.

**CPI — the inflation report (released this morning, 8:30am ET)**
The June Consumer Price Index (CPI) — the government's main measure of how fast prices are rising — came in much cooler than expected:

| Measure | Actual | Expected |
|---|---|---|
| Headline MoM | -0.4% | -0.2% |
| Headline YoY | 3.5% | 3.8% |
| Core MoM (ex food & energy) | 0.0% | +0.2% |
| Core YoY | 2.6% | 2.9% |

This is the biggest monthly price decline in more than six years. The main driver: gasoline prices fell 9.7% in June. Core inflation (stripping out energy and food swings) was flat on the month, the lowest reading in some time.

This is objectively good news. It reduces the immediate pressure on the Fed to raise rates. It tells us the inflation spike earlier this year is not accelerating.

The concern: some of that relief may reverse. Iran tensions have already pushed oil prices back up in July. A single cool month does not guarantee inflation is beaten. The Fed's own preferred measure of inflation still sits at 4.1%, well above its 2% target.

**Valuation (how expensive is the market?)**
The S&P 500's trailing price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio is approximately 25.7x as of July 13. The long-term historical median is around 18x. The forward P/E — based on projected earnings over the next year — is around 20x. By either measure, the market is priced for strong future performance, leaving little room for disappointment.

The 10-year Treasury yield (the "risk-free" rate investors can earn by lending to the US government for 10 years) sits near 4.59%. That is meaningful competition for stocks. Investors who can earn 4.59% safely are less willing to pay a high price for uncertain equity returns.

**Interest rates / the Fed**
The Federal Reserve's benchmark short-term interest rate is currently 3.50-3.75%, unchanged for several consecutive meetings. The next rate decision is July 29, 2026.

Before today's CPI, markets were pricing roughly a 50% chance of a rate hike at that meeting — a sharp jump from under 10% a few weeks ago. The cool CPI print likely brings that probability down somewhat, but the Warsh testimony (see below) keeps uncertainty high.

---

## The big event: Fed Chair Warsh testimony (today and tomorrow)

Kevin Warsh became Federal Reserve Chair in May 2026. Today (10am ET, House Financial Services Committee) and tomorrow (10am ET, Senate Banking Committee) are his first congressional testimony sessions — the first time the public hears his full views on inflation, rates, and the economy.

Key points from his prepared remarks:
- The Fed has "no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation."
- He gave **no signal** on whether a rate hike is coming at the July 29 meeting.
- Roughly half of FOMC members expect a rate hike by year-end; roughly half do not.
- Warsh called AI investment "the most striking feature of the economy right now."

What this means: we still do not know if rates will go up in two weeks. An unexpected rate hike is the single biggest near-term risk to stock prices.

## Bank earnings — Q2 2026 season begins

Today was day one of major bank earnings. JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Goldman Sachs, Wells Fargo, and Citigroup all reported Q2 2026 results this morning. JPMorgan posted its largest quarterly profit on record, beating estimates significantly ($6.14 EPS vs $5.85 expected; $58 billion revenue vs $50 billion expected). Wall Street bank stocks were mixed despite the strong results — a pattern typical of "buy the rumor, sell the news."

This is the start of earnings season. More large companies report over the coming weeks. Strong earnings can support elevated valuations; disappointing earnings would be a headwind.

## What this means for us today

Hold all cash. The cool CPI is welcome news but does not change the setup: the market is at historically high valuations, a Fed rate hike in two weeks is still possible, and none of our names are at prices where we require a margin of safety.

The one mild positive shift from yesterday: the two biggest near-term uncertainties (CPI and Warsh tone) have partially resolved in a constructive direction. If tomorrow's PPI is also cool, and if Warsh's Senate testimony is not more hawkish, the rate-hike fear could fade. That would be the setup to watch heading into next week.

For now: watch, do not act. The 200-day trend remains up. We are being patient, not pessimistic.
