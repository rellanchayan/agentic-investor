# Market Weather — 2026-07-09

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The market is 7.8% above its long-term average and trading at stretched valuations while a U.S.-Iran war is pushing oil prices sharply higher, the 10-year Treasury yield sits near a 4-week high at 4.57%, and a critical June inflation reading arrives next Tuesday — conditions that demand a larger discount than any of our candidates currently offer.

## What SPY is doing

**SPY** (the fund holding the 500 largest US companies — our benchmark) closed Wednesday at roughly $748. That sits **7.8% above its 200-day moving average of $694**. A price above the 200-day average says the market is in a general uptrend. That is a mild positive, but being 7.8% above that line also means the market is elevated relative to its own recent trend.

This morning (Thursday July 9), **S&P 500 futures are pointing up about 0.3%**, suggesting a modest rebound after Wednesday's decline of 0.28%. But the intraday picture is mixed: the Dow fell 1.1% on Wednesday while the Nasdaq gained 0.2%, driven by AI and semiconductor names. Yesterday's split — tech up, industrials and blue chips down — reflects investors rotating rather than showing uniform confidence.

## What happened yesterday that matters: FOMC Minutes

The Federal Reserve (the US central bank) published the minutes from its June 17 meeting yesterday (July 8) at 2:00pm ET. These are the detailed notes of what the 19 rate-setting officials discussed.

The headline: **officials remain divided on rate direction**. The good news — and it is mild good news — is that most participants said the fed funds rate would be "unchanged or slightly below" its current level of 3.50–3.75% by year-end. This is a touch less alarming than the June 17 dot plot, which showed 9 of 19 officials favoring a rate hike. The bad news is that the committee is explicitly split and new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh withheld his own rate projection, leaving the market without a clear signal from the person who matters most. The minutes carry the message: *we will wait for more inflation data before deciding anything*.

That data arrives next Tuesday.

## Interest rates

The **10-year US Treasury yield** — the rate the government pays to borrow money for 10 years, and the baseline against which stock valuations are implicitly compared — is sitting at **4.57%**, near a 4-week high.

When the 10-year yield is elevated, it competes with stocks for investor money and makes it harder to justify paying high prices for future corporate earnings. At 4.57%, the pressure on valuations is real. The Fed's short-term policy rate (3.50–3.75%) is unchanged, but the market-driven long-term rate is creeping higher.

---

## Iran war and oil

Overnight, the US launched fresh strikes against Iran and President Trump indicated no interest in negotiations. Iran responded by targeting Gulf facilities. This is an ongoing armed conflict, and the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil passes — is in the middle of it.

The result: **Brent crude oil surged 5.4% to $78.19 per barrel** over the last session, following a large jump the day before. WTI (the US benchmark) settled around $73.52. Energy company stocks (ConocoPhillips, Marathon Petroleum) rallied on the oil spike, but the broader market did not celebrate — higher oil means higher gasoline prices, which means more inflation pressure, which means the Fed has less room to cut rates.

---

## Inflation (CPI) — the big event is Tuesday

**CPI (Consumer Price Index)** measures how fast everyday prices rise. The Fed's target is 2% per year.

The most recent reading is May 2026: **headline CPI was 4.2% year-over-year** — the highest in three years. The Cleveland Federal Reserve's real-time nowcasting model projects June 2026 CPI at **approximately 3.96% year-over-year**. That would be a step down from 4.2%, but still nearly double the Fed's 2% target.

The **June 2026 CPI report releases Tuesday July 14 at 8:30am ET**. This is the single most important piece of macro data this week and next. A hot reading (above 4%) would likely hurt stocks meaningfully, as it would strengthen the case for rate hikes at the July 29 FOMC meeting. A cool reading (below 3.5%) would provide relief. The range of uncertainty is wide.

The New York Federal Reserve's consumer survey, reported this week, shows **one-year inflation expectations near three-year highs**. This matters because the Fed worries about "unanchored" expectations — if people believe inflation will stay high, it becomes self-fulfilling through wage demands and pricing behavior.

## Is the market expensive or cheap?

**Expensive by historical measures, roughly fair by near-term earnings estimates.**

- **Trailing P/E ratio** (what investors pay per dollar of last year's earnings): approximately **25 times** earnings. The long-run historical median is about 18. You are paying a premium.
- **Shiller CAPE ratio** (a 10-year smoothed version that avoids one-year distortions): approximately **39.9**, which is about **125% above the historical average**. This is the more conservative and longer-term measure, and it says the market is significantly elevated.
- **Forward P/E** (based on estimated future earnings): approximately **20 times**, which is near the 5-year average. This is the most optimistic framing — it assumes earnings will grow strongly, which is possible if AI and the economy cooperate.

The summary: the market is not obviously cheap on any measure, and it is expensive on the measures that look back at realized earnings rather than hopeful forecasts.
