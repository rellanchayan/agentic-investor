# Market Weather — 2026-06-26

**Posture: CAUTIOUS**

The market is selling off this morning on AI valuation fears, inflation remains well above the Fed's 2% target, and valuations by long-run measures are elevated. Good companies are not on sale today. Demand the full margin of safety before any buy.

---

## What SPY is doing

SPY closed yesterday at $732.33. Its 200-day moving average — the average price over roughly the last 200 trading days, a widely-used indicator of whether the market is in a broad uptrend or downtrend — is $690.53. SPY is about 6% above that average, which means the long-term trend is still pointing up.

However, this morning S&P 500 futures are down about 0.5%, Nasdaq futures are down more sharply (~1.25%), driven by a broad tech sell-off. Today is a risk-off day, not a crash, but it is not the kind of morning where bargains are obvious.

---

## What the Fed is doing

The Federal Reserve held its interest rate unchanged at the June 16-17 meeting (target range: 3.50–3.75%). More importantly, the Fed removed its previous language signaling rate cuts this year and indicated that a rate hike is now possible. The next FOMC meeting is July 28-29.

What this means: borrowing costs are staying high for longer than the market hoped earlier in 2026. That is a headwind for stock valuations, particularly for expensive growth stocks.

---

## Inflation

The most recent CPI reading (May 2026) shows prices rising 4.2% over the past year — more than double the Fed's 2% goal. The PCE index (the Fed's preferred inflation measure) shows 4.1% year-over-year as of May. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) is at 2.9%.

Energy prices are actually falling today — oil dropped about 3.8% as tankers resumed movement through the Strait of Hormuz — but the underlying inflation picture remains uncomfortable. The next CPI release is July 14.

---

## Market Valuation

- The trailing P/E ratio of the S&P 500 is approximately 25–31 (sources differ on calculation method). The forward P/E is about 22.
- The Shiller CAPE ratio — a smoothed, inflation-adjusted earnings measure over 10 years — is 39.7, which is 23% above its long-term average. This is in historically elevated territory and is the most conservative signal.
- In plain terms: the market is not obviously cheap. Paying full price for stocks in an expensive market with sticky inflation and a hawkish Fed is not consistent with our "quality at a fair price" discipline.

---

## What is driving today's move

OpenAI is reportedly considering delaying its IPO to 2027, preferring to wait for a $1 trillion valuation rather than list sooner at a lower price. This has reignited fears that the entire AI investment cycle has been priced on hype rather than profits. Semiconductor and AI-adjacent stocks are broadly down today. South Korea's KOSPI fell 5.8% on June 23 in a global tech contagion episode.

None of this breaks the thesis on any specific quality business — but it is a reminder that tech valuations are fragile when earnings expectations are questioned.
