# Market Weather — 2026-07-07

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The S&P 500 is technically in an uptrend but is priced at a significant premium to history while inflation is running at 4.2% year-over-year and a newly-hawkish Federal Reserve is signaling possible rate hikes rather than cuts — conditions that demand a larger discount before we deploy capital.

## What the market is doing right now

**SPY** (the fund that holds the 500 largest US companies — our benchmark) closed Friday at $745.98 and opened this week after the July 4 holiday. That price sits about 7.6% above its **200-day average** of $693.15. A price above the 200-day average tells us the market is in a general uptrend, which is a mild positive.

However, this morning S&P 500 futures are dipping roughly 0.1–0.25%. The drag comes from semiconductor stocks: Samsung reported quarterly profits that surged 19-fold, but traders wanted even more, and the stock fell as much as 11% in Seoul. US chip-adjacent names like Micron and Sandisk are down over 4% in premarket trading. The Dow did set a new record close yesterday (53,055) and the S&P 500 gained 0.72% on July 6, so the very short-term backdrop is a bit mixed — strong yesterday, weaker today.

## Interest rates and the Fed

The **Federal Reserve** (the US central bank) controls short-term interest rates. Higher rates make it more expensive for companies to borrow and typically push stock prices down; lower rates do the opposite.

At its June 17 meeting, the Fed held its target rate at **3.50–3.75%** for the fourth meeting in a row. That sounds stable, but the tone has shifted in a hawkish direction. The meeting was the first under new Fed Chair **Kevin Warsh**, and the updated dot plot (the Fed's own forecast of where rates are headed) shows that **9 of 19 officials now expect at least one rate hike this year**, and 6 expect at least two. This is a meaningful shift from a "hold and wait" stance toward a potential tightening bias. Markets had been hoping for rate cuts; they are now having to price in the possibility of increases instead.

---

## Inflation

**CPI (Consumer Price Index)** measures how fast everyday prices are rising. The Fed targets 2% per year.

The most recent reading is for May 2026: headline CPI rose **4.2% year-over-year** — the highest reading in three years — driven in part by energy prices and supply shocks. The three-month annualized pace of headline inflation was running at roughly 8.2%, an even more alarming near-term rate. Core CPI (which strips out food and energy) is at 2.82% year-over-year — closer to normal, but still above the Fed's target. Until inflation comes down convincingly, the Fed has a reason to keep rates high or raise them further.

## Is the market expensive or cheap right now?

Expensive. The **price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio** of the S&P 500 — a standard measure of how much investors are paying for each dollar of corporate earnings — is currently estimated at roughly 25 to 32 times earnings depending on the method used (trailing vs. forward). The long-run historical median is around 18 times. At least one valuation model estimates the market is approximately 76% above historically normal levels.

None of this means a crash is coming. Markets can stay expensive for years, and earnings growth can eventually "grow into" a high P/E. But it does mean that the margin of error is thin. Any negative surprise — a hot CPI, a hawkish Fed comment, a disappointing earnings season — hits harder when prices are high.
