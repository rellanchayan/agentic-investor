## Interest Rates and Inflation

The US 10-year Treasury yield is at 4.68% — near a two-month high. When the yield on a very safe government bond is nearly 4.7%, investors ask themselves: "Why take stock-market risk for an uncertain return when I can earn nearly 5% on Treasuries?" That pressure tends to push stock prices down, especially for richly valued companies. The Fed has not cut rates, and the market does not expect one next week (the FOMC is widely expected to hold steady).

Inflation risk has ticked up this month because of oil. Brent crude spiked to briefly above $100/barrel on Thursday (driven by Middle East escalation; see below). It is retreating today, but higher energy prices feed into everything from transportation to manufacturing costs. Core PCE inflation is expected to remain above 3%.

---

## Geopolitics

The US-Iran ceasefire that was in place earlier this year has collapsed. Houthi rebels attacked Saudi oil tankers this week. President Trump threatened to strike Iranian infrastructure if attacks on Strait of Hormuz shipping continued; Iran threatened to retaliate against US-linked energy assets. This is the main reason oil briefly hit $100 and why energy markets remain jittery. A further escalation could push oil higher again.

On trade: the Trump administration announced 10–12.5% tariffs on 60 trading partners this week. That adds a new layer of economic uncertainty — higher input costs, potential retaliation from trading partners, and margin pressure for US exporters.

## Are Stocks Expensive or Cheap Right Now?

The S&P 500 is trading at about 20.3 times the next twelve months' expected earnings (the "forward P/E"). For context:
- The 5-year average forward P/E is 19.9x
- The 10-year average is 19.0x
- On realized past earnings (trailing P/E), the multiple is ~25x
- The Shiller CAPE (a smoothed long-run measure) is ~39x — historically very expensive

The market is not wildly overpriced on a forward-earnings basis, but it is above average, and those earnings forecasts depend heavily on AI spending producing returns that have not yet shown up clearly in the numbers. After this week's tech earnings, that assumption looks riskier.
