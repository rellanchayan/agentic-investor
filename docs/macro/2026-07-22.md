# Market Weather — 2026-07-22

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The market is expensive, oil is surging due to an active US-Iran military conflict, the Fed meets in six days with a hawkish inflation stance, and every name with a thesis is priced above its fair value — nothing meets the bar for buying today.

---

## What each term means (quick reference)

- **SPY**: a fund that holds the 500 largest US companies. It is our benchmark.
- **200-day average**: the average closing price of SPY over the last ~200 trading days. SPY above it means the market is in an uptrend; below it means a downtrend.
- **Forward P/E**: the price of the S&P 500 divided by the earnings analysts expect companies to earn over the next 12 months. The higher it is, the more expensive the market is.
- **Fed / FOMC**: the US central bank. It sets short-term interest rates. Higher rates make borrowing costlier and tend to push stock prices down.
- **CPI / PCE**: measures of how fast prices are rising (inflation). The Fed wants inflation near 2%.

## Where things stand this morning

### Stock market trend
SPY closed Tuesday at **$747.08**, which is **7.1% above** its 200-day moving average of $697.83. The trend is technically up. However, being this far above the long-run average is a sign of extension — the market has run a long way and already prices in a lot of good news.

Pre-market futures this morning are slightly negative: S&P 500 futures down ~0.2%, Nasdaq futures down ~0.6%, Dow roughly flat. The market came off a strong Tuesday (chip stocks rallied, S&P snapped a 3-day losing run) and is now pausing ahead of major earnings tonight.

### The big event tonight: Alphabet and Tesla earnings
Alphabet (GOOGL) and Tesla (TSLA) report Q2 2026 results after the close today — the first two of the "Magnificent Seven" megacap tech companies to report this earnings season. Analysts expect Alphabet revenue of ~$101 billion with continued cloud growth; Tesla produced over 450,000 vehicles in Q2. These are massive companies, and a surprise in either direction can move the whole market tomorrow morning. We are watching, not acting.

### Geopolitics and oil
The US-Iran military conflict is in its **10th consecutive day of strikes**. The US has struck over 80 targets inside Iran, including Kharg Island — which handles roughly 90% of Iran's oil exports. The US has also reimposed oil sanctions on Iran.

The result: oil prices are surging again. WTI crude is near **$87-88 per barrel** (up roughly 3-4% today), and Brent crude is near **$94-95 per barrel**. This is the fourth consecutive session of oil gains. Higher oil is inflationary, adding to the Fed's problem.

### Interest rates and the Fed
The Federal Reserve's next meeting is **July 28-29**, exactly six days away. The rate decision lands on **July 29 at 2:00pm ET**. Markets widely expect the Fed to hold rates unchanged.

But the tone is hawkish. Under new Chair Kevin Warsh, the Fed recently raised its PCE inflation projection to **3.6%** (from a prior 2.7% estimate) — well above the 2% target. The stance is "higher for longer": rates stay elevated until inflation comes down, and that may take longer than the market hoped. High rates are a headwind for stock valuations.

### Inflation
The most recent CPI reading: **3.5% year-over-year** (June 2026). Well above the Fed's 2% target. Rising oil prices make the path back to 2% harder.

### Market valuation
The S&P 500's forward P/E ratio (price vs. expected next-12-months earnings) is approximately **20.3x**. That is above the 5-year average of 19.9x and above the 10-year average of 19.0x. The market is not wildly expensive, but it is not cheap either — it is priced for things to go well. The trailing P/E is ~25x, which is more concerning. When you buy at elevated valuations, the margin for error is thin.

### Tariff risk
President Trump announced new tariffs this week: 50% on Canadian imports, a proposed 100% tariff on imported generic drugs, and 25% on Brazilian goods effective today. Tariffs raise consumer prices, add to inflation, and complicate the Fed's job.
