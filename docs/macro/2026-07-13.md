## What the numbers say

**SPY vs its 200-day average**
SPY closed Friday at $751.50. Its 200-day average (the average closing price over the last ~200 trading days) is $694.90. That means SPY is about 8% above the average, which signals a healthy long-term uptrend. The structural trend is still up.

However, being above the 200-day average also means the market did not get cheap. It got more expensive while we held cash.

**Futures this morning**
US stock futures are pointing lower heading into Monday's open. The prediction market is pricing only a 22% chance the S&P 500 opens higher today. Asian equities have reacted sharply — South Korea's Kospi fell roughly 7% overnight. This is a risk-off morning.

**Valuation (how expensive is the market?)**
The S&P 500's trailing price-to-earnings ratio is approximately 24-25x. Forward P/E is around 20x. Putting those numbers in historical context: the market is sitting at roughly the 89th percentile of all valuations recorded since 1871. In plain English, only about 1 in 10 readings in history have been more expensive than today. This is not a "sale" environment.

**Interest rates**
The Federal Reserve's benchmark rate is 3.50%-3.75%, held unchanged for four consecutive meetings through June 2026. Rates are not falling. The market is pricing roughly a 25% chance of a further hike at the upcoming July 28-29 FOMC meeting. Inflation is still running above the Fed's 2% target. Higher rates make future corporate earnings worth less today — this is a mild headwind for stock valuations.

---

## The big event: US-Iran military exchange (this weekend)

Over the weekend of July 12-13, US forces and Iranian forces exchanged strikes. The flash point is the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway between Oman and Iran through which roughly 20% of the world's oil flows. Both sides are disputing whether the strait remains open to shipping.

What this means for markets:
- **Oil is up ~4%.** Brent crude is around $78-79/barrel. WTI is around $73-74. If the strait is disrupted, energy prices could go higher. Higher energy prices feed into inflation.
- **Stocks are under pressure** because geopolitical conflict creates uncertainty, and uncertainty makes investors demand a higher return (lower prices) before owning stocks.
- For a long-term investor, a geopolitical shock is not automatically a reason to sell anything we own. But it is a reason to demand a bigger discount before buying something new. We do not want to catch a falling knife while the situation is still unresolved.
