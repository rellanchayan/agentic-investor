# Market Weather — 2026-06-30

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

One-sentence reason: the market is sitting 7.7% above its 200-day average and is expensive by
long-run valuation measures, inflation is running well above the Fed's target, and a cluster of
major economic reports (JOLTs today, ISM and ADP mid-week, Jobs Report Thursday) could move
prices sharply before the week is out.

---

## What "cautious" means for us

We do not panic and we do not sell. We hold our cash patiently and demand a bigger-than-usual
discount before pressing the buy button. Specifically, a name needs to trade meaningfully below
our base fair value — not just near it — before we act. Today no candidate on our watchlist
meets even the normal margin-of-safety threshold, so this posture changes nothing practically;
it simply confirms that holding cash is the correct call.

## Key facts this morning

### SPY and the 200-day average

SPY closed yesterday at 744.75. Its 200-day average (the average of the last ~200 daily closing
prices) is 691.39. That means SPY is 7.7% above its long-run trend line — a sign that the
market is in an uptrend and not in a state of fear or deep discount. This is a mild caution
flag: when the market is well above its own trend, it is harder to find truly cheap prices.

### Futures this morning

S&P 500 futures are slightly positive (~7,455, up a little from yesterday's close). Nasdaq 100
futures are up about 0.4%. The open is expected to be quiet rather than dramatic.

### Valuation — is the market cheap or expensive?

- Trailing P/E ratio: approximately 25.3. This is right at the long-run historical average
  (~25.3), so on this simple measure the market looks fairly valued.
- Shiller CAPE ratio: 39.64 as of June 2026. This is a "smoothed" P/E that averages earnings
  over ten years to filter out short-term swings. It is 23% above its own long-term average of
  about 32. By this measure, the market is meaningfully expensive. Long-run expected returns
  from elevated CAPE levels are historically below average.

Bottom line: not a crash-level valuation, but not a bargain either. A quality investor should
demand a real discount to fair value before buying — and that discount is harder to find here.

### Interest rates and the Fed

The Federal Reserve's benchmark interest rate is currently 3.50%–3.75%, held steady at the
June 17 meeting under Chair Kevin Warsh. The next FOMC meeting is July 28–29. Markets are
pricing in a 79.5% probability of no change at that meeting.

Higher rates (3.5%+ vs. near-zero in 2021) mean that the "discount rate" applied to future
corporate earnings is elevated, which justifies lower stock prices than we might have expected
a few years ago. The market has so far shrugged this off, but it remains a structural headwind.

### Inflation

- CPI (Consumer Price Index, May 2026): up 4.2% year-over-year.
- Core PCE (the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, May 2026): up 3.4% year-over-year.
- Both are running well above the Fed's 2% target. This means the Fed is unlikely to cut
  rates anytime soon, which keeps the cost of borrowing elevated for both businesses and
  consumers.

### Earnings season

We are not in the middle of earnings season right now. Most Q1 2026 reports are behind us.
Q2 2026 earnings season begins in earnest in mid-July.
