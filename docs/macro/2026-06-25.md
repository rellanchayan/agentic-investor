# Market Weather — 2026-06-25

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** Inflation just printed at a three-year high, the Fed's own forecasts
lean toward at least one more rate hike before year-end, and the broad market is still trading
at elevated valuations — so we keep demanding a meaningful discount before putting any money to work.

---

## What the numbers say this morning

**SPY and its 200-day average**
SPY (the fund that tracks the 500 largest US companies, and our benchmark) last traded at
$735.24. Its 200-day average — the average price over roughly the last 200 trading days, a
rough measure of whether the market is trending up or down — sits at $690.12. SPY is about
6.5% above that average. That is a healthy uptrend, which is a mild positive. But it also
means we are not getting a broad market discount right now; the market has climbed well above
its own long-run trend line.

**Futures this morning**
S&P 500 E-Mini futures are up about +0.78% and Nasdaq 100 E-Mini futures are up about +2.15%
in pre-market trading. The driver is a single-company event: memory chipmaker Micron reported
earnings last night that far exceeded expectations (adjusted EPS of $25.11 vs. $20.78 forecast),
and its stock jumped roughly 17%. That enthusiasm is lifting AI- and semiconductor-adjacent
names and pulling the Nasdaq higher. It is real good news for the chip sector but it is not a
statement about the broad economy.

**Valuations — is the market cheap or expensive?**
Two standard measures both say the market is pricey:
- Trailing P/E ratio (price divided by the past year of earnings): roughly 25–32 depending on
  the data source. The long-run historical average is around 15–20.
- Shiller CAPE ratio (a smoothed, inflation-adjusted version averaging 10 years of earnings):
  approximately 41, well above its own long-run average of around 32.

Neither number signals an imminent crash, but both say stocks in general are not cheap. Our
founding principle is clear: when the market is expensive, we require a larger discount before
buying any individual company.

**Inflation — the big print today**
The Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge, the PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)
index for May, was released this morning at 8:30am ET:
- Headline PCE: +4.1% year-over-year — the highest reading since April 2023.
- Core PCE (stripping out food and energy): +3.4% year-over-year.
Both readings came in roughly in line with forecasts, so there was no nasty surprise.
But the numbers themselves are unwelcome: the Fed's target is 2%, and we are running at twice
that pace. The main culprit is energy prices, which remain elevated because of conflict in
Iran disrupting oil supplies.

**Interest rates and the Fed**
The Federal Reserve held its short-term interest rate steady at 3.50%–3.75% at its most recent
meeting on June 17. That meeting was the first chaired by Kevin Warsh, the new Fed Chair.
The Fed's updated "dot plot" (a chart of each committee member's interest rate forecast) had
nine of eighteen members penciling in at least one rate hike before the end of 2026. The
median year-end forecast for rates is now 3.75%–4.00%, a quarter-point above where we are today.
Money markets are currently pricing in a near-certain 25-basis-point hike by October 2026.

What does this mean in plain English? Higher rates make it more expensive for companies to
borrow and expand, and they make bonds more attractive relative to stocks. A rate hike that
the market has not fully priced yet is a mild headwind for stock valuations. With hot inflation
giving the Fed cover to hike, we should assume rates go up rather than down in the near term.

## What this means for us today

The posture is cautious, and it is the correct call. The Micron-led futures rally this morning
is encouraging news for AI hardware, but it does not change the macro facts: inflation is at
a three-year high, the Fed is leaning toward hiking, and the broad market is trading at
elevated valuations. Our candidates do not offer enough of a discount to justify deploying cash
into a choppy, expensive, rising-rate environment.

In practice, cautious means:
- We do not buy just because futures are green. A single blowout chip-company report is not
  a reason to pay a premium price for a different business.
- We continue to read and research. Q2 earnings season starts next month, and fresh numbers
  from AAPL, MSFT, and V may either confirm or break our current fair-value estimates.
- If any of our candidates pull back toward their fair value — MSFT to the $330s, V to $250s,
  AAPL to below $180 — we revisit immediately.
- Cash earns nothing but costs nothing. Being patient here is disciplined, not passive.
