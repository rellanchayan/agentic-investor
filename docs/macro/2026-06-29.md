# Market Weather — 2026-06-29

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The market is expensive by every valuation measure, the Fed is leaning toward raising rates (not cutting them), inflation is running well above target, and a market-moving jobs report lands on Thursday — taken together, these conditions call for demanding a bigger-than-normal discount before buying anything.

---

## What SPY Is Doing

SPY closed Friday at 736.67. Its 200-day average price is 690.93. That means today's price is about 6.6% above the long-run trend line — a sign that the market is technically in an uptrend, not a downtrend. That is the one genuine piece of good news: the broad market is not in free-fall.

Futures this morning are pointing up. S&P 500 futures are +0.8% and Nasdaq futures are +1.2% in premarket. The driver is a geopolitical relief: the US and Iran agreed over the weekend to halt hostilities over the Strait of Hormuz, easing fears about oil supply and broader conflict. Technology stocks are also bouncing — they were hit hard last week (the Nasdaq fell 4.6% and the S&P 500 fell 2%), so some recovery is natural.

**What this means for us:** A single morning bounce does not change the bigger picture. We are long-term investors; we do not chase a green open.

---

## How Expensive Is the Market?

Two valuation measures both say the same thing: the market is expensive.

- **Shiller CAPE ratio: 39.64** — This is 23% above the long-term average of about 32. The CAPE (Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings) smooths out short-term profit swings by looking at 10 years of earnings. A reading this high has historically been followed by below-average long-term returns, though it tells you nothing about the next six months.
- **Forward PE: 22.06** — Analysts expect the S&P 500 to earn certain profits over the next year; at today's prices, you pay $22 for every $1 of expected earnings. The long-run median is around $18. So you are paying a premium.

**What this means for us:** When the market is expensive, good companies tend to be expensive too. We need to find names trading meaningfully below their fair value before deploying our $5,000 daily budget. This is not a signal to never buy — it is a signal to be selective and demand a real discount.

## Interest Rates and Inflation

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate steady at 3.50-3.75% at its June 17 meeting (the first under new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh). But the tone shifted: the Fed removed language suggesting future cuts and its internal forecasts now show nine officials expecting at least one rate hike in 2026, with cuts pushed to 2027-2028.

Why? Inflation is not cooperating:
- **Core PCE (the Fed's preferred inflation measure): 3.4% year-over-year** as of May 2026. This is the highest reading since October 2023, and it is running well above the 2% target. Month-over-month it re-accelerated to 0.3%.
- **Overall PCE: 4.1% year-over-year** — energy prices from the Middle East conflict are adding fuel to the fire.

Higher rates make borrowing costlier for businesses and consumers, which tends to slow growth and put downward pressure on stock prices — especially on expensive, growth-oriented companies. The direction of travel here is not favorable.

## Big Events This Week

Two events this week could move the whole market:

1. **Wednesday, July 1 — Fed Chair Warsh speaks (9:30am ET).** Any hint that rate hikes are more likely, or that the Fed is less patient with inflation, would be negative for stocks.

2. **Thursday, July 2 — June Jobs Report (NFP, 8:30am ET).** Released one day early ahead of the July 4 holiday. Consensus expects +172,000 new jobs. A strong number (say, 220,000+) could push rate-hike odds higher and pressure stocks. A weak number could ease those fears. This is the single biggest market-moving data point of the week.

We are not in earnings season right now, so there are no major company earnings reports to worry about.

---

## Geopolitical Note

The US-Iran situation remains fluid. A ceasefire was announced over the weekend, which is why futures are up this morning. However, the situation in the Middle East has been driving energy price spikes and contributing to the PCE inflation numbers. If hostilities resume, oil prices would jump and inflation expectations would rise further. This is a tail risk, not a base case — but worth noting.

## What This Means for Us Today

We hold no positions and have the full $5,000 daily budget available. That is a clean slate.

The cautious posture does not mean "do nothing forever." It means:
- Any buy candidate needs to be trading at a meaningful discount to our fair-value estimate — more than the normal margin of safety.
- Looking at our watchlist in context.json: MSFT is at $375.98 against a base fair value of $378 — essentially at fair value, no margin of safety (MOS of 0.55%). V is at $344.99 against a base fair value of $297 — above fair value, negative MOS. AAPL is well above fair value (negative 44% MOS). None of these currently meet even a normal margin of safety, let alone a cautious one.
- Cash held because nothing meets the bar is disciplined. The playbook is clear on this.
- Watch Thursday's jobs report before committing to any new position this week. If the market sells off on a strong number, that could improve entry prices on quality names.

**Today's posture: CAUTIOUS. Hold cash. No new buys unless a candidate trades at a substantial discount to fair value.**
