## What happened yesterday and what the market is doing this morning

Yesterday (June 17) was ugly. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq dropped sharply after the Federal
Reserve's first meeting under new Chair Kevin Warsh delivered a hawkish surprise: rates were
held steady, but the Fed's own forecasts ("dot plot") removed any expectation of a cut and
suggested a hike is possible before the end of 2026. Markets did not like hearing that.

This morning (June 18), stocks are bouncing back. The S&P 500 is up roughly 1.15%, the
Nasdaq is up about 1.5%, and tech stocks are leading. The Russell 2000 (small companies) is
modestly down, which tells us the rally is not fully broad — investors are choosing selectively.
S&P 500 E-Mini futures were up roughly 0.85% in pre-market, pointing to a positive open.

**Important:** markets are closed tomorrow, Friday June 19, for the Juneteenth federal
holiday. Today is the last trading day of this shortened week. Any limit orders placed today

## SPY and its 200-day average

SPY (the fund that holds the 500 largest US companies, and our benchmark) is at $746.34.
Its 200-day moving average — the average closing price over roughly the past 200 trading
days, a rough measure of whether the market is in a longer-term uptrend or downtrend — sits
at $688.33.

SPY is about 8.4% above that average. Being above the 200-day is healthy: it says the market
is in an uptrend, not in a breakdown. But being nearly 9% above the average also means there
is no deep discount in the index itself right now. Patient buyers are not getting a gift from
Mr. Market at this level.

## Interest rates and the Federal Reserve

The Fed's policy committee (FOMC) met on June 16–17 and voted unanimously (12-0) to hold the
federal funds rate steady at 3.50%–3.75%. This was the fourth consecutive meeting with no
change.

The surprise was in the Fed's updated forecasts. Under new Chair Kevin Warsh (who took over
from Jerome Powell), the committee:

- Removed prior language suggesting future cuts were likely.
- Published a dot plot in which 9 of 18 officials project at least one rate hike before the
  end of 2026.
- Raised their inflation forecast for 2026 sharply — they now expect PCE inflation (their
  preferred measure) to run at 3.6% this year, up from a prior estimate of 2.7%.

What this means in plain English: the Fed is telling the market "we are not cutting rates and
we might actually raise them." Higher rates make borrowing more expensive for companies and
consumers, and they push stock valuations down by making future profits worth less in today's
dollars. This is a headwind for equities, especially for high-valuation growth stocks.

## Inflation

The most recent Consumer Price Index reading (CPI for May 2026, released June 10) came in at
**4.2% year-over-year**. That is more than double the Fed's 2% target. The breakdown matters:
- Energy prices surged 23.5% over the past year — the primary culprit.
- Core CPI (which strips out food and energy) ran at 2.9%, which is elevated but more moderate.
- Shelter (rent and equivalent) contributed 3.4% annually.

The Fed's own preferred inflation gauge, PCE, ran at 3.8% for April. The next PCE reading
(for May) is due June 25 — one of the most important data points this month.

## The geopolitical wildcard: US-Iran peace deal

A US-Iran interim peace framework was announced in mid-June, with a formal signing ceremony
expected on June 19 in Switzerland. This matters because the Iran conflict had disrupted oil
tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway that handles a large share of
global oil shipments. The prospect of that reopening sent WTI crude oil down about 4.8% to
roughly $80.83 per barrel.

Cheaper oil is a direct path toward lower inflation. If the deal holds, it removes the
biggest single driver of the 4.2% CPI reading. That would eventually ease pressure on the Fed.

However: the deal has not yet been formally signed as of this writing. Geopolitical agreements
can fall apart. If the June 19 signing is delayed or collapses, oil prices would likely spike
back toward $88–$95, and the inflation picture darkens again.

This is a genuine positive development — but it is a "we'll believe it when we see it" item,
not something we bake into our posture until the ink is dry and oil stays down.

## Are stocks cheap or expensive right now?

The S&P 500's trailing price-to-earnings ratio (P/E — price divided by the past year of
actual earnings) is approximately 25.7. The historical long-run average is around 15–20.
The forward P/E (price divided by next year's expected earnings) is about 20.1, above its
own 10-year average of 19.0.

Neither ratio signals a crash, but they do say: the market is not cheap. Stocks in general
are priced for a fairly optimistic future. When you pay a full price for earnings, you need
those earnings to actually materialize — and with inflation at 4.2% and the Fed considering
rate hikes, earnings estimates face real headwinds.

For us as long-term investors, this means: we need individual companies to be priced below
their intrinsic value, not just "less expensive than the overall market." Our playbook says
we must demand a margin of safety — a real discount to fair value — before buying anything.

## What this means for us today

We are cautious. This is not a panic — the market's primary trend is intact (SPY above its
200-day average), the economy is not in recession (jobless claims are running near 226,000,
which is healthy), and the Iran peace deal offers a real path toward lower energy prices.

But we do not chase bounces. A green morning after a red afternoon does not make stocks
cheaper. Our three thesis names are not at buy levels. The Fed has just told us rates may
go higher. Inflation is more than double the target. The market P/E is elevated.

In practice, cautious means:
- Use the $5,000 daily budget only if a thesis name reaches its buy-eligible price.
- None of them are there today. We hold cash.
- We continue researching new names (GOOGL, NVDA, and others on the candidate list have
  no theses yet — that research work can proceed without deploying money).
- We watch PCE on June 25 closely. A cool reading could move the posture toward normal.
- We do not place orders today that we would regret if the Iran deal collapses over the
  weekend and oil spikes Monday morning.

The playbook reminder: "A missed buy costs nothing; an overpriced buy costs money."
