# Market Weather — 2026-06-17

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

**One-sentence reason:** The Fed held rates today but delivered a hawkish surprise — the dot
plot now pencils in only one small cut for the rest of 2026, bond yields jumped, and futures
markets are now pricing a meaningful probability of a rate *hike* by December, which is a
materially more difficult backdrop for already-expensive equities.

## What the numbers say this morning

**SPY and its 200-day average**

SPY (the fund holding the 500 largest US companies, our benchmark) last traded at $750.25.
Its 200-day average — the average of the past ~200 trading days, which tells us the broad
trend — sits at $687.85. SPY is about 9.1% above that average. Being above the 200-day line
is a healthy signal: the market is still in an uptrend. But being nearly 10% above it means
there is no meaningful discount built in at the index level.

**Market direction this morning**

S&P 500 futures were slightly soft in pre-market. Technology shares came under renewed pressure
after semiconductor stocks (Nvidia, Broadcom, AMD, Intel) sold off sharply on Tuesday. The S&P
500 finished Tuesday down about 0.6%, while the Dow eked out a small gain. The market is in a
holding pattern ahead of the 2:00pm ET Fed announcement and Warsh press conference.

**The big event today: FOMC decision and Kevin Warsh's press conference**

This is the single most important event of the week, arriving at approximately 2:00pm ET today.
Here is what has happened and what it means:

- The Federal Reserve voted 10-2 to hold its short-term interest rate steady at 3.50%–3.75%.
  This was fully expected — futures markets had priced a 97%+ chance of no change.
- What was *not* fully expected was the hawkish tone. The updated "dot plot" (the committee's
  own forecast of where rates will go) now shows only one 25 basis-point cut remaining for all
  of 2026, compared to earlier projections that implied more easing.
- New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh (sworn in on May 22, this was his debut press conference) signaled
  that the Fed is in no hurry to cut. His tone reinforced the message: rates stay higher for longer.
- Bond markets reacted immediately: the 10-year Treasury yield jumped to around 4.6%.
- Fed-funds futures swung sharply — some estimates now put the probability of a rate *hike* by
  December 2026 at roughly 70%. The question in the market has shifted from "how many cuts?" to
  "could rates go higher?"

Higher rates for longer matter because they raise the discount rate used to value stocks. When
the risk-free rate rises, every company's fair value falls a little. This is one reason we
demand a margin of safety before buying.

**Valuations — is the market cheap or expensive?**

The market remains expensive by historical standards:

- Shiller CAPE ratio (a smoothed, inflation-adjusted measure averaging 10 years of earnings): 
  approximately 40.5–41.4 as of June 2026. The long-run average of this measure is around 32, so
  the market is trading roughly 26% above its own historical norm. The all-time record high was
  44.2 (set in 1999).
- Trailing P/E ratio: roughly 25–32 depending on the index and the source. The long-run average
  is around 15–20.

Neither of these numbers means a crash is coming. Markets can stay expensive for a long time.
What they mean for us is simple: there is very little "built-in cushion" in the index. When you
buy a stock in an expensive market, any error in your own fair value estimate hurts more, because
the whole environment is priced for perfection.

**Inflation**

The most recent CPI reading (May, released June 10) was 4.2% year-over-year. Core PCE — the
Fed's preferred inflation gauge — is running at approximately 3.1%. Both are well above the
Fed's 2% target. Hot inflation is bad for stocks in two ways: it pressures the Fed to keep rates
high, and it erodes the real value of future earnings.

**The Iran deal and oil**

Last weekend, President Trump announced a preliminary US-Iran peace agreement that would reopen
the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint for roughly 20% of global crude shipments. US crude oil fell
about 4.8% to ~$80.75 per barrel on the announcement (international Brent fell similarly to
~$83). Cheaper oil is good news because energy prices have been one of the main drivers of the
4.2% CPI reading.

However, oil is still up roughly 40% since the start of 2026, and the deal is preliminary.
Geopolitical agreements can fall through. This is a genuine positive for the inflation picture
but not yet a game-changer for the Fed's calculus.

## What this means for us today

We remain CAUTIOUS. This posture is actually a small step more defensive than yesterday, because
the Fed's hawkish surprise has nudged the rate outlook in a direction that is unfavorable for
stock valuations. The market is above its 200-day average (healthy trend), but:

- Valuations are elevated (CAPE ~40+)
- Inflation is hot (CPI 4.2%)
- The Fed is now signaling higher-for-longer, with futures markets pricing a possible rate hike
- All our watched names are trading above their fair values

CAUTIOUS posture in practice means:
- We hold cash. Holding cash earning ~3.5% (risk-free) while waiting for quality businesses to
  go on sale is not a failure — it is the plan.
- We continue reading and refreshing research. Knowing our prices in advance means we are ready
  to act quickly when the market does pull back.
- We do not chase. A stock going up is not a reason to buy it. The entry price is part of the thesis.
- The key pre-set buy levels (from yesterday's decision doc) remain our targets: MSFT near $310,
  V near $243. These are not imminent, but they are the right prices to wait for.

No orders today unless something unusual appears at our price levels.
