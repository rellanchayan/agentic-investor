## What the numbers say

**SPY (our benchmark):** Closed at $746.85, which is 7.9% above its 200-day average of $692.27. The 200-day average is the mean closing price over the past ~200 trading days. When the current price is above it, the market is in a general uptrend — not a crash, not a panic. That is the one positive signal today.

**S&P 500 index level and valuation:** The index is near 7,500, up about 0.25% on the day. The P/E ratio (the price investors pay per dollar of corporate earnings) stands at approximately 26.8. To put that in context: the long-run historical median since 1871 is around 18. At 26.8, the market sits at roughly the 89th percentile of all historical readings — meaning the market has been cheaper than this about 89% of the time in history. This does not mean a crash is coming, but it does mean prices have less cushion against bad news.

## The big event that already happened today

The **June nonfarm payrolls report** was released at 8:30am ET this morning (moved up from the usual first-Friday slot because markets will be closed Friday July 3 for the Independence Day observed holiday). The result: the US economy added only **57,000 jobs** in June, well below the consensus forecast of ~113,000. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2%.

**Why this matters:** Weak job growth tells the Fed the economy may be cooling, which reduces the pressure to raise interest rates further. Markets interpreted this as mildly good news — the probability of a September rate hike fell from about 63% to about 51% according to CME FedWatch. Stock prices rose modestly on this read.

**The catch:** The Fed's current rate (3.5-3.75%) is still historically elevated. Lower rates tend to support higher stock prices; higher rates do the opposite. With a hike still a coin-flip and inflation not yet back to the Fed's 2% target (as of the June meeting statement), rates are not clearly falling yet.

## Interest rates and the Fed

The Fed held its benchmark rate at **3.50-3.75%** at its June 2026 meeting (unanimous 12-0 vote). New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh noted inflation remains elevated relative to the 2% goal. The next scheduled rate decision is **July 28-29 (FOMC meeting)**. Today's soft jobs number reduces but does not eliminate the chance of another hike.
