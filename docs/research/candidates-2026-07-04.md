## Candidates Proposed

### 1. META — Meta Platforms (Communication Services)

**The business in two sentences:** Meta owns Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Messenger,
serving roughly 3.54 billion daily active users across its family of apps. It sells digital
advertising into that audience, generating some of the highest operating margins in large-cap
technology.

**The moat:** Network effects are the moat — your friends and family are on these platforms,
and switching costs rise the more of your social and professional life is embedded. Competing
at this scale of distribution is effectively impossible for a new entrant.

**Smell test:**
- Profitable: Yes. Q1 2026 operating margin ~40%.
- Free cash flow: ~$48.4 billion for full-year 2026, up ~5% vs 2025. Q1 2026 FCF alone was
  $16.2 billion (+46% YoY). Real, large, consistent cash generation.
- Balance sheet: Net cash positive.
- Liquidity: Among the most liquid U.S. equities, billions in daily dollar volume.
- Understandable: Yes — advertising revenue tied to user engagement.

**Honest caveat:** Reality Labs (VR/AR hardware) is losing roughly $17 billion per year and
has accumulated over $73 billion in cumulative losses since 2021. This is a genuine drag and
a strategic bet that could either pay off or continue to burn capital. The core advertising
business is exceptional; Reality Labs is the risk to watch.

**Gap it fills:** Communication sector — gives us the leading social media franchise alongside
DIS (traditional media).

### 2. BLK — BlackRock (Financials)

**The business in two sentences:** BlackRock is the world's largest asset manager, overseeing
approximately $13.9 trillion in assets under management (AUM) as of Q1 2026, spanning ETFs
(iShares), institutional mandates, and alternative investments. It also licenses its Aladdin
risk-management technology platform to hundreds of financial institutions, creating a second
revenue stream.

**The moat:** Scale and switching costs, both. At $14 trillion AUM, BlackRock has cost
advantages in manufacturing ETFs (iShares is the global leader) and brand trust that
institutional clients rely on. The Aladdin platform is deeply embedded in client workflows —
a large bank rebuilding its risk infrastructure away from Aladdin is a multi-year, high-risk
project, so clients stay.

**Smell test:**
- Profitable: Yes. Q1 2026 revenue +27% YoY to $6.7 billion; record results noted in filings.
- Free cash flow: $4.7 billion for full-year 2024 (+23% vs 2023), with analysts forecasting
  further growth toward $12 billion by 2028 as HPS acquisition fees compound.
- Balance sheet: Asset-light, fee-based model; no significant leverage.
- Liquidity: NYSE-listed, large-cap, easily tradeable.
- Understandable: Yes — asset managers earn a percentage fee on assets they manage. Bigger AUM
  means more revenue, and AUM grows with markets and net inflows.

**Honest caveat:** Fee rates compress over time as passive ETFs commoditize; BLK must keep
winning active and alternative mandates to offset this. Also, AUM shrinks in bear markets
(fees are based on asset values), so revenue is market-sensitive.

**Gap it fills:** Asset management — a different moat type than our existing banking (JPM)
and payments (V, MA) names. Diversifies the financials bucket meaningfully.

### 3. MCO — Moody's Corporation (Financials)

**The business in two sentences:** Moody's rates the creditworthiness of debt issued by
corporations and governments worldwide (Moody's Investors Service), and separately sells
risk-analytics and workflow software embedded into bank lending, insurance underwriting, and
KYC compliance processes (Moody's Analytics, $3.6 billion in recurring ARR). When companies
or governments want to borrow in capital markets, they need a Moody's (or S&P) rating — there
is no practical substitute.

**The moat:** A genuine regulatory duopoly with S&P Global on the ratings side (two firms
dominate global credit ratings with decades of track record), plus deep switching costs on the
analytics side. In Q1 2026, Moody's rated over $2 trillion in debt issuance. Even when bond
markets close — as they did for eight months in 2022 — the company's return on invested
capital held at 14.5%, demonstrating how durable the business is across cycles.

**Smell test:**
- Profitable: Yes. FCF margins exceed 33%; return on equity 62.1%.
- Free cash flow: $2.8–$3.0 billion guided for full-year 2026. Q1 2026 FCF +26% YoY to
  $844 million. Highly consistent.
- Balance sheet: Carries some debt (ratings agencies are capital-light but do lever up), but
  the FCF generation comfortably services it.
- Liquidity: NYSE-listed, mid-to-large cap, liquid.
- Understandable: Yes — issuers pay to have their bonds rated; subscribers pay for analytics
  software.

**Honest caveat:** Ratings revenue is cyclical — when interest rates are high and debt issuance
slows, Moody's Investors Service revenue falls. However, the analytics segment (recurring ARR)
provides a buffer, and the 2022 experience showed the floor is higher than feared. Also, MCO's
market cap (~$80 billion) is smaller than the other names on this watchlist; still very liquid.

**Gap it fills:** Financial data and credit infrastructure — we own no financial-data business.
The moat type (regulatory entrenchment + switching costs) is additive to our watchlist and
different from banking or payments.

## Honorable Mention — AMZN (Not Added This Week)

Amazon passed the moat test (AWS is the cloud leader, marketplace has strong network effects,
logistics is hard to replicate) and the scale/liquidity test easily. However, reported trailing
twelve-month free cash flow collapsed to roughly $1.2 billion (some measures show it
slightly negative depending on how lease obligations are treated) because Amazon is spending
approximately $200 billion per year in capital expenditures for AI infrastructure and data
centers. Full-year 2025 FCF was $11.2 billion — better, but still well below where it has
been historically. Operating income is strong ($23.9 billion in Q1 2026 alone), so this looks
like a deliberate investment cycle rather than structural deterioration. The analyst should
revisit AMZN once there is clearer evidence that FCF is recovering toward 2025 levels or
above. The business is clearly watchlist-worthy; the timing of adding it is the question.
