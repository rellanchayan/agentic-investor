## What the business does (plain English)
JPMorgan Chase is the largest U.S. bank. A bank earns money two ways: the **spread** between
the interest it pays on deposits and the interest it earns on loans/securities (net interest
income), plus **fees**. JPM has four engines:
- **Consumer & Community Banking** — checking/savings, the #1 U.S. credit-card business (Chase),
  auto loans, home lending for tens of millions of households.
- **Commercial & Investment Bank** — M&A advice, underwriting stocks and bonds, trading, and
  treasury/payments for corporations and governments.
- **Asset & Wealth Management** — managing money for institutions and wealthy people (fees on assets).
- **Corporate/Treasury** — the balance sheet and funding function.

FY2025 reported **total net revenue $182.4B** and **net income $57.0B ($20.02 diluted EPS)**.

## Why "gross margin / FCF / debt" don't apply — and what to use instead
Banks are not normal companies, so the usual ratios mislead:
- **Gross margin → N/A.** Use **ROTCE** (return on tangible common equity), the **efficiency
  (overhead) ratio**, and **net interest margin** instead.
- **ROIC / ROE → use ROTCE.** JPM reports it directly: **20% in FY2025** (ROE 17%). Above the
  ~10% cost of equity, so it compounds tangible book value.
- **Net-debt/EBITDA → N/A** (debt is a raw material for a bank). Use the **CET1 capital ratio**:
  **14.5%** at 2025-12-31, well above regulatory minimums (~11–12% incl. buffers). Fortress capital.
- **Free cash flow → N/A.** Proxy: **$57.0B net income** and a **$1.50/share quarterly dividend**
  (4Q25) — profitable and returns capital.

## Why it has a moat (a wide one)
- **Funding-cost advantage:** the industry's largest low-cost, sticky deposit base — cheap raw material.
- **Scale:** #1 in U.S. cards, top-tier investment banking and payments; the largest tech budget in banking.
- **Switching costs & trust:** primary bank/payment relationships are sticky; a fortress balance sheet wins deposits in stress.
- **Regulatory scale:** the compliance burden that hurts small banks is a barrier that protects the giants.

I rate the moat **wide**.

## Fair value (bank-appropriate: P/E + P/TBV blend)
The code did the arithmetic. Two standard bank lenses:
- **P/E:** $20.02 EPS × **12 / 14 / 16** → **$240 / $280 / $320**. Even a premium bank is cyclical;
  12–16x through the cycle is generous for JPM, which historically trades ~11–14x.
- **P/TBV:** $107.56 TBVPS × **2.2 / 2.7 / 3.0** → **$237 / $290 / $323**. A base of 2.7x is what a
  20% ROTCE justifies via (ROTCE − g)/(r − g) with g = 4%, r = 10%. Top-quality banks trade ~1.8–2.5x;
  JPM earns a premium.

**Blended fair value: low $238 · base $285 · high $322.**

At **$336.38**, JPM trades **above** the high end of the band — about **−18% margin of safety**
(the price is ~3.1x tangible book and ~17x earnings, near the top of its historical range).
Verdict: a wonderful business, but **expensive today**. This is a watch-and-wait, not a back-up-the-truck price.

## The real risks (as hard as the bull case)
- **Valuation:** no margin of safety; we'd be paying a rich premium to tangible book.
- **Cyclicality:** FY2025 net income fell ~2% vs FY2024; a recession spikes card/loan loss provisions fast.
- **Rate sensitivity:** net interest income moves with rates and deposit repricing.
- **Regulation:** higher capital rules (Basel III endgame) or stress-test outcomes can cap buybacks.
- **Tail/credit risk:** a leveraged balance sheet can be hit hard by a severe credit or market shock.
- **Leadership transition** risk over time.

## Sell triggers (what would break the thesis)
1. ROTCE below 12% for two+ consecutive quarters.
2. CET1 (standardized) below 12%.
3. Reported total net revenue shrinks year-over-year for two consecutive fiscal years.
4. Net charge-offs / provisions spike far above guidance (a credit break, not normal cyclicality).
5. Dividend cut or buybacks suspended by regulators for capital weakness.
6. A serious accounting restatement, control failure, or governance red flag.

## Bottom line
JPM is a wide-moat, best-in-class bank earning a 20% ROTCE on a fortress balance sheet — a business
we would happily own. But at $336 it sits ~18% above our base fair value with no cushion. Conviction
**3/5**: high on the franchise, tempered by a rich price and the inherent cyclicality of banking.
The buy case opens up nearer the **$238–285** band.
