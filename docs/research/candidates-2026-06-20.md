## Candidates Proposed

### 1. MCO — Moody's Corporation

**Business in one line.** Moody's rates the creditworthiness of bonds and lends its ratings to every bond prospectus, investor mandate, and regulatory filing that cites them, then sells analytics (Moody's Analytics) as a second segment.

**Moat in one line.** A regulatory oligopoly: only ten NRSROs exist in the US, Moody's and S&P together handle roughly 80% of rated issuance, and any issuer who wants their bond bought by pension funds, index funds, or bank portfolios must obtain a rating from one of these two — there is no substitute.

**Smell test.**
- Scale and liquidity: market cap approximately $79 billion as of May 2026; highly liquid NYSE stock.
- Profitability: 2025 revenue $7.72 billion (+8.9% year-on-year); net income $2.46 billion (+19.5%); operating margins consistently 45-50%.
- FCF: the business is capital-light (no factories, no inventory); margins translate to substantial free cash flow.
- Balance sheet: manageable debt for an asset-light business; returns capital via buybacks and dividends.
- Outlook: management guided 10-14% EPS growth in 2026 and high-single-digit revenue growth.
- 10-year durability: debt markets existed for centuries; rating agencies embedded in global regulation are not going away.

**Sector gap filled.** Financials — adds a capital-markets / data angle alongside V, MA, JPM.

**What would need to be true for a full thesis.**
- Verify net debt / FCF ratio is comfortable (not covered in this scouting pass).
- Quantify how much revenue is tied to debt issuance volumes (cyclical) vs. recurring subscription (stable).
- Estimate a fair value range using earnings power and DCF; compare to current ~$430 stock price.
- Confirm no material litigation risk from legacy rating-agency settlements that could recur.

**What to watch for.** A prolonged credit-issuance drought (recessions reduce bond issuance), or a regulatory change stripping NRSRO status or mandating fee caps.

### 2. LMT — Lockheed Martin Corporation

**Business in one line.** The world's largest defense contractor by revenue: designs and manufactures fighter jets (F-35), missiles, missile-defense systems, satellites, and classified programs, almost entirely for the US government and allied militaries.

**Moat in one line.** A regulatory and technical oligopoly: only a handful of primes can build a 5th-generation stealth fighter or an ICBM; the F-35 platform alone underpins 27% of revenue and its support tail runs through the 2070s; switching costs for a sovereign customer committed to a weapons system are effectively infinite.

**Smell test.**
- Scale and liquidity: market cap approximately $131-141 billion as of June 2026 (stock ~$511); very liquid.
- Profitability: 2025 revenue $75.0 billion (+5.7% year-on-year); net earnings $5.0 billion; operating profit $7.7 billion.
- FCF: Q3 2025 FCF $3.3 billion, Q4 2025 FCF $2.8 billion — strong cash generation supporting dividends and buybacks.
- Backlog: $160+ billion as of 2025, roughly 2x annual revenue, providing exceptional earnings visibility.
- Balance sheet: long-term debt ~$18-19 billion. This is meaningful, but the business generates $6-7 billion in operating cash annually, so coverage is solid. The debt is rated investment grade.
- 10-year durability: governments do not stop needing fighter jets, missile defense, and satellites. NATO members are increasing defense budgets.

**Sector gap filled.** Industrials — adds aerospace and defense alongside CAT (heavy equipment), HON (industrial conglom), and UNP (rails).

**What would need to be true for a full thesis.**
- Stress-test revenue assuming a 10-15% US defense budget cut (how does the backlog hold?).
- Clarify the F-35 production ramp trajectory and any per-unit margin pressure.
- Build a fair-value model: the stock fell from ~$672 (March 2026 high) to ~$511 now — determine whether that creates a margin of safety or reflects a real fundamental concern.
- Check pension liability, which can be a hidden drag for large defense primes.

**What to watch for.** Federal budget sequestration or continuing-resolution risk; F-35 delivery schedule slippage; any rotation in US policy away from legacy manned aircraft toward drones/autonomous systems.

### 3. SHW — The Sherwin-Williams Company

**Business in one line.** Sherwin-Williams makes and sells paint and coatings across more than 6,700 company-owned stores in North America, selling overwhelmingly to professional painting contractors who return for the same products on every job.

**Moat in one line.** A combined brand and distribution moat: professional painters pick a brand in training and rarely switch (switching mid-project risks color mismatches and different drying times); 6,700 owned stores give Sherwin-Williams reach no rival can match without decades and billions of capital.

**Smell test.**
- Scale and liquidity: market cap approximately $79 billion (stock ~$314 as of mid-June 2026); liquid NYSE large-cap.
- Profitability: Q1 2026 net sales $5.67 billion (+6.8%); diluted EPS $2.15 (+7.5%); 2025 operating cash $3.45 billion (14.6% of net sales).
- 2026 full-year guidance: diluted EPS $10.70-$11.10; adjusted EPS $11.50-$11.90. Company reaffirmed guidance after Q1.
- 10-year durability: paint is inescapable — every building is painted and repainted; professional painting is not automatable.

**Caution flag — leverage.** Sherwin-Williams carries approximately $10.9 billion in total debt against modest equity, resulting in a debt-to-equity ratio that is high by most standards (2x-4.6x depending on the precise calculation). This is a deliberate capital allocation choice (high ROE via leverage) but it means the company is sensitive to refinancing risk if rates stay elevated, and limits balance-sheet flexibility. This is the most important thing a full thesis must address.

**Sector gap filled.** Materials — the first and only entry in that sector on the watchlist.

**What would need to be true for a full thesis.**
- Model the debt maturity schedule and refinancing cost at current rates.
- Estimate what a 20% housing turnover decline does to volume (the biggest near-term risk).
- Build a fair-value estimate; the stock is well off prior highs, which may or may not reflect a real opportunity.
- Confirm that the Professional Stores segment (highest-margin) remains dominant vs. the consumer/DIY segment.

**What to watch for.** Housing starts and existing home sales volumes; interest rate trajectory affecting both housing and debt refinancing; any significant market share gain by PPG or RPM.
