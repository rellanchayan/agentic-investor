## Candidate 1 — SPGI (S&P Global Inc.)

**Sector gap filled:** Financials — financial data, credit ratings, and index licensing

### Business (two sentences)
S&P Global provides credit ratings (through S&P Ratings), financial intelligence and data
(Market Intelligence), index licensing (S&P Dow Jones Indices — the brand that every S&P 500
ETF pays to use), and commodity/energy data (Commodity Insights / Platts). It earns money
primarily by acting as a mandatory gatekeeper: bond issuers must pay to get rated, and ETF
managers must pay to license the S&P 500 brand.

### Moat (one sentence)
SPGI and Moody's together form a government-blessed near-duopoly in credit ratings — ratings
that are legally required for most institutional bond sales — creating switching costs and pricing
power that are essentially structural features of global capital markets.

## Candidate 2 — TJX (The TJX Companies, Inc.)

**Sector gap filled:** Consumer Discretionary — off-price / value retail

### Business (two sentences)
TJX is the world's largest off-price retailer, running T.J. Maxx, Marshalls, HomeGoods,
HomeSense, and Sierra across 5,000+ stores in nine countries. It buys excess inventory,
cancelled orders, and manufacturer overruns from more than 21,000 global vendors and sells
them to shoppers at 20-60% below typical department-store prices.

### Moat (one sentence)
TJX's global sourcing scale — buying $56B+ of merchandise per year opportunistically from
21,000+ vendors — makes it the first call for any brand with surplus stock, and that purchasing
power compounds with store count, creating an advantage no smaller competitor can replicate
(Morningstar rates it wide moat).

## Candidate 3 — ODFL (Old Dominion Freight Line, Inc.)

**Sector gap filled:** Industrials — less-than-truckload freight / domestic transportation

### Business (two sentences)
Old Dominion runs the largest LTL (less-than-truckload) trucking network in the United States,
consolidating small shipments from thousands of businesses into efficient routes through its
service-center network and delivering them with industry-leading reliability. Unlike truckload
carriers, LTL is a dense, hub-and-spoke business where scale, network coverage, and service
quality compound over time to drive pricing power.

### Moat (one sentence)
ODFL has been rated the #1 National LTL Carrier for Quality for 15 consecutive years, achieves
a 99% on-time delivery rate and 0.1% cargo claims ratio, and operates a terminal network built
over decades that a new entrant cannot credibly replicate — together these create freight density
advantages that drive sustainably higher margins than peers.

### Quick smell test
| Check | Result |
|---|---|
| Profitable? | Yes — CY2025 net income ~$1.024B (cyclically weaker; -14% from freight-cycle peak but firmly positive) |
| Free cash flow positive? | Yes — LTM FCF ~$955M (OCF $1.37B minus capex $415M) |
| Revenue | ~$5.5B CY2025 |
| Q2 2025 operating ratio | 74.6% (elevated vs. 71.9% in Q2 2024 due to freight downturn, but still profitable) |
| Cyclical note | LTL tonnage per day fell ~9% in Q2 2025; revenue down 6%. This is freight-cycle softness, not a structural break. ODFL maintained pricing discipline (+5.3% revenue/cwt ex-fuel). |
| Market cap | ~$44B (NASDAQ: ODFL, July 2026) |
| Avg daily dollar volume | ~$350M per day (1.6M shares × ~$215) — well above $50M threshold |
| Plain ticker? | Yes — ODFL on NASDAQ |
| Moat? | Network density, service quality reputation, pricing power over peers |
| Understandable? | Yes — moves freight more reliably than rivals, charges for the privilege |

All checks pass. Flag for the analyst: ODFL is in a freight-cycle trough; numbers look
weaker than peak but the moat is intact. A full thesis should model through-the-cycle
normalized earnings, not just the current trough.

## What we are NOT proposing and why

Several plausible names were considered but set aside:

- **MCO (Moody's):** Excellent business, nearly identical moat to SPGI. We picked SPGI first
  because it is larger and adds the index-licensing angle. MCO is worth adding later.
- **BLK (BlackRock):** Tremendous asset manager and ETF platform, but we decided the financials
  sub-sector gap is best plugged by SPGI first given its deeper regulatory moat.
- **ORLY (O'Reilly Automotive):** High-quality auto-parts retailer with strong switching costs.
  Healthy but not as globally dominant as TJX; could be a future addition.
- **Insurance (PGR, CB):** The insurance sector is entirely uncovered. These require a different
  analytical framework (combined ratio, underwriting cycles). Worth a dedicated scouting pass.
