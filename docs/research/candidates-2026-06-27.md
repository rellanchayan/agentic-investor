## Executive Summary

The watchlist has good coverage of technology, payments, consumer staples, and healthcare pharma.
Three gaps stand out:

1. **Financial data and exchanges** — We own JPM, V, MA (transaction processors and a bank) but have
   no exposure to financial information infrastructure. SPGI and its peers (MCO, MSCI, ICE) run the
   data pipes that every financial firm in the world depends on. These are among the highest-quality
   recurring-revenue businesses ever built.

2. **Medical devices / surgical platforms** — Our healthcare names are pharmaceutical (LLY, ABBV, JNJ)
   and managed care (UNH). We have no exposure to medical devices or life-sciences tools, which carry
   a very different risk profile (less patent-cliff exposure, more hardware and consumables).

3. **Life sciences tools** — The enabling layer underneath biotech and pharma R&D. Companies like TMO
   supply instruments, reagents, and services to every drug developer on earth. Revenue is stickier
   than pharma because customers can't easily switch out an embedded analytical platform mid-study.

Three candidates are proposed below: **SPGI**, **ISRG**, and **TMO**. Each passed a quick smell test
on moat, profitability, liquidity, and understandability. All concerns are noted honestly.

## Candidate 1: S&P Global (SPGI)

### The Business

S&P Global runs four businesses: Ratings (credit ratings on bonds and issuers), Market Intelligence
(financial data and analytics for investors), Commodity Insights (energy and commodities data via
Platts), and Indices (the S&P 500 and thousands of other benchmarks that trillions of dollars track).
Every time a bond is issued, a financial model is built, or an ETF tracks a benchmark, S&P Global is
likely collecting a fee. Revenue is predominantly subscription and transaction-based.

### The Moat

Wide moat. Three reinforcing advantages:

- **Regulatory quasi-duopoly in ratings**: Credit ratings from S&P and Moody's are embedded in
  regulations, bond covenants, and investment mandates worldwide. A new entrant cannot simply decide
  to compete — issuers need ratings from an NRSRO, and S&P has been one for decades. Morningstar
  assigns SPGI a wide moat rating (cited below).
- **Switching costs in data and indices**: Over 94.3% client retention in Market Intelligence in 2024
  (S&P Global earnings release). Index licensing is even stickier — a fund cannot easily change its
  benchmark without triggering tax events and investor confusion; the S&P 500 is essentially a
  permanent standard. GuruFocus assigns SPGI a moat score of 8/10 as of May 2026.
- **Intangible assets**: 95%+ of revenue comes from proprietary data, benchmarks, and ratings that
  cannot be replicated cheaply. The brand trust behind a credit rating took a century to build.

### Current Price and Market Cap

### Why It Belongs on the Watchlist

SPGI earns an adjusted operating margin of 50% — a level only achievable when customers truly cannot
leave. It generates roughly $5.5B of free cash annually from a largely fixed-cost, subscription base.
It is in a sector we have zero exposure to (financial data infrastructure). At ~20x forward earnings
after a ~30% decline from its 52-week high, the research analyst should assess whether the price
has moved closer to a fair entry range.

### Concerns

- Debt load of $12.4B was taken on partly to fund the 2022 IHS Markit merger; at 1.5x EBITDA it is
  manageable but it is real.
- Ratings revenue is cyclical — if bond issuance slows (high-rate environment), Ratings can decline.
- Regulatory risk: if governments change how ratings are mandated, the duopoly could erode.
- Valuation has historically been premium; even at current levels it is not obviously cheap.

**Sector gap filled:** Financial data / information services (new sub-sector, not currently covered)

## Candidate 2: Intuitive Surgical (ISRG)

### The Business

Intuitive Surgical makes the da Vinci robotic surgical system, used in minimally invasive soft-tissue
surgery. The company sells the robot itself (capital sale) and then earns recurring revenue from
single-use instruments and accessories (consumed per procedure), service contracts, and training.
Over 11,000 da Vinci systems are installed globally as of year-end 2025; more than 12 million
procedures have been performed to date. This is a classic "razor and blade" model — the installed base
creates a durable, predictable stream of high-margin consumable revenue that grows every year
procedures are performed.

### The Moat

Wide moat. Multiple reinforcing layers:

- **Installed base lock-in**: A hospital that has trained its surgeons on da Vinci, built its OR
  scheduling around it, and received regulatory approval for specific procedures has essentially no
  practical reason to rip out the system and start over with a competitor. Motley Fool (May 2026)
  describes the moat as "getting stronger" as new da Vinci 5 placements accelerate.
- **Training infrastructure**: Former executives identify surgeon training as the core moat — not the
  hardware. It takes years to train surgical teams, and that training is specific to the da Vinci
  ecosystem.
- **Data flywheel**: 12M+ procedures generate proprietary outcomes data that feed AI/ML surgical
  guidance tools. This data advantage compounds as more procedures are performed.
- **Regulatory barriers**: Each new surgical indication requires FDA clearance. ISRG has accumulated
  hundreds of cleared indications over 25 years; a new entrant would need a decade to replicate this.
- **Market share**: Intuitive holds roughly 70–80% share in soft-tissue robotic surgery worldwide.

### Key Financials (all figures from cited SEC filings and earnings releases)

|---|---|
| Revenue | $10.1B (+21% YoY) |
| Net income | $2.9B (28.4% net margin) |
| Free cash flow | $2.5B (nearly doubled from $1.3B in 2024) |
| Cash and investments | $9.0B (at year-end 2025) |
| Long-term debt | Minimal; net cash position |
| Systems placed in 2025 | 1,721 da Vinci units |

Source: Intuitive Surgical Q4 2025 8-K (SEC filing, January 2025); Yahoo Finance earnings highlights;
Macrotrends FCF data; Motley Fool analysis May 2026.

### Current Price and Market Cap

- Price: ~$405 (as of June 26, 2026; 52-week range ~$397–$604)
- Market cap: ~$143B
- P/E (TTM): ~49x — premium multiple
- Daily dollar volume: high (large cap, Nasdaq-listed)
- Note: stock is down approximately 28% year-to-date (from above $600), which the research analyst
  should scrutinize closely — is this a sentiment-driven decline or a fundamental deterioration?

### Why It Belongs on the Watchlist

ISRG is one of very few businesses in the world where the moat actively grows the more customers use
the product. Every new system placed deepens training ties, adds procedures to the data flywheel, and
expands the consumables revenue stream. The balance sheet is fortress-grade: $9B in cash with minimal
debt. Revenue grew 21% in 2025 with FCF nearly doubling. The stock's 28% YTD decline creates a
possible window where the research analyst should examine whether valuation has become more reasonable.
We have no medical devices exposure at all — this fills that gap in healthcare.

### Concerns

- Valuation: ~49x P/E is still a high multiple that requires sustained high growth to justify.
- Competition: J&J (Ottava), Medtronic (Hugo), and others are investing heavily in competing platforms.
  The moat is real but it is under attack in a way it was not five years ago.
- Single-product dependency: nearly all revenue ties back to the da Vinci ecosystem.
- The 28% YTD decline and 52-week low of ~$397 suggest the market has real concerns — the analyst
  must identify exactly what has changed.

**Sector gap filled:** Medical devices / surgical robotics (no prior exposure in health care devices)

## Candidate 3: Thermo Fisher Scientific (TMO)

### The Business

Thermo Fisher Scientific is the world's largest life sciences tools and services company. It supplies
instruments, reagents, consumables, software, and contract research/manufacturing services (CDMO) to
pharmaceutical companies, biotech firms, academic labs, hospitals, and governments. Think of it as
the "arms dealer" to drug developers: whoever wins the race to develop the next drug, they likely
used Thermo Fisher equipment and reagents to get there. Revenue of $44.6B in 2025 makes it one of
the largest healthcare companies in the world by sales.

### The Moat

Wide moat, built primarily on switching costs and scale:

- **Switching costs**: Once a lab has validated its workflow on a Thermo Fisher instrument platform,
  switching is extremely costly. FDA-regulated drug development requires validated methods; changing
  supplier mid-study can trigger re-validation and regulatory delays that no pharma company wants.
  Multi-year CDMO contracts and software/service agreements add further lock-in.
- **Scale**: With $44B in revenue and 125,000 employees, TMO can offer bundled enterprise agreements
  that smaller competitors simply cannot replicate. A pharma company can negotiate a single contract
  covering instruments, reagents, software, and manufacturing services.
- **Intangible assets and regulatory approvals**: GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) certifications
  and regulatory filings take years to obtain. TMO's network of GMP-certified facilities is a
  meaningful barrier.
- GuruFocus assigns TMO a moat score of 8/10.

### Current Price and Market Cap

- Market cap: ~$191B
- P/E (TTM): ~28x; Forward P/E: ~20x
- Analyst consensus: Buy; average 12-month target ~$596 (StockAnalysis, June 2026)
- Daily dollar volume: highly liquid

### Why It Belongs on the Watchlist

TMO earns $6.3B in free cash flow annually — real, recurring cash generated from supplying every
major life sciences company on earth. Its customers are not choosing TMO for price; they are choosing
it because switching is too costly and risky. Revenue grew modestly in 2025 (biotech funding was
tight post-2021), but the structural demand for life sciences research tools only grows with aging
populations and expanding global R&D investment. The stock is down ~20% from its 52-week high.
We have no exposure to life sciences tools or instruments — TMO fills this gap cleanly.

### Concerns

- Debt: Net debt of approximately $28B is the largest concern. TMO deployed $13B in M&A in 2025
  alone. At net debt / FCF of roughly 4-5x, this is elevated. The analyst must assess whether the
  debt trajectory is declining and whether FCF is sufficient to service it comfortably.
- Revenue growth has slowed: 4% in 2025 vs. historical high-single-digit growth, partly because
  the COVID-related revenue tailwinds (PCR testing, vaccine manufacturing) have fully faded.
- Biotech funding cycles matter: if venture/biotech funding tightens, academic and emerging biotech
  lab spending can soften.
- At $191B market cap, TMO is already a large position for any portfolio — meaningful sizing will
  require conviction.

**Sector gap filled:** Life sciences tools and services (no prior exposure in healthcare enabling layer)
