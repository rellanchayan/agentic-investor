# Market Weather — 2026-07-23

## Posture: CAUTIOUS

One-sentence reason: The US-Iran ceasefire has collapsed, Brent crude has surged ~34% in July to ~$98, the 10-year Treasury yield is at a two-month high of ~4.64%, the FOMC meets in six days, and none of our thesis candidates trade at a margin-of-safety discount.

---

## What the signals say

### SPY and the 200-day average
SPY closed at $738.76. Its 200-day average (the average of the last ~200 closing prices) is $698.18. SPY is about 5.8% above that average, which means the broad market is still in an uptrend. That is the one clearly bullish data point today.

### Futures this morning
US stock futures are pointing lower before the open. S&P 500 futures are down roughly 0.3% to 1.1%; Nasdaq futures are down 0.3% to 2.2%. The spread between the two sources is wide, but both agree the direction is negative. The Nasdaq weakness is led by disappointing post-earnings reactions to Tesla (fell on margin compression) and Alphabet (fell despite a revenue beat because management guided to $195-205B in AI capital spending).

### Interest rates / the Fed
The Federal Reserve's current short-term rate is 3.50-3.75%. The FOMC meets July 28-29; the rate decision comes Wednesday July 29 at 2:00 PM ET. Markets currently price in no change. Even so, the meeting adds uncertainty — any surprise statement or change in language about inflation can move markets sharply.

Meanwhile the 10-year Treasury yield is near a two-month high at roughly 4.63-4.68%. Higher long rates make stocks less attractive relative to bonds, and they raise the cost of borrowing for companies we might want to own.

### Oil and geopolitics
This is the primary risk driver today. The US-Iran ceasefire that briefly stabilized energy markets has broken down. Three tankers were attacked in the Strait of Hormuz between July 17-19. The Strait carries roughly 20% of the world's oil supply, and traffic has dropped sharply (only 8 ships transited on one day last weekend, versus normal flows of many dozens). Iran has stated there are no active negotiations.

Brent crude is near $98 per barrel, up approximately 34% for the month of July — one of the three largest monthly gains in a decade. The last time we assessed the market (July 13 posture) oil was up roughly 4% on this conflict. It has now risen far more. Higher oil raises inflation expectations, which could push the Fed to hold rates higher for longer.

### Market valuation
The S&P 500's forward price-to-earnings ratio is approximately 20.3x. That is above the 5-year average of 19.9x and the 10-year average of 19.0x. The good news is that earnings growth has been strong (roughly 88% of reporting companies beating expectations, with projected 2026 earnings growth of ~24.5%), which has actually brought this ratio down 7.7% from the start of the year. But the market is not cheap. When the market is not cheap and risks are rising, a careful investor demands more discount before buying.

### Earnings season
We are in the peak of Q2 earnings season. Alphabet and Tesla reported after the close on July 22; both stocks fell despite mixed-to-decent results because expectations were high. Intel, Honeywell, and Blackstone report Thursday. Earnings season creates more daily price volatility than usual, which means our prices move around more — but it does not change whether a business is fundamentally attractive.

### Investor sentiment
AAII (the American Association of Individual Investors) sentiment survey shows bulls fell from 44.9% to 29.6% week-over-week, while bears rose to 42.3%. This is a meaningful shift toward fear. Contrarian theory says that extreme fear can precede a market bottom. We note it but do not act on it alone — sentiment can stay bearish for months while prices keep falling.

## What this means for us today

Hold cash. Do not buy anything. The environment is actively less favorable than it was ten days ago: oil has surged, the ceasefire has collapsed, rates are rising, and futures are negative. None of our candidates offer a margin of safety even under normal conditions, let alone cautious ones.

The FOMC meeting on July 29 is the next meaningful event to watch. If the Fed signals concern about oil-driven inflation, rates could rise further, pushing stock prices lower — and potentially bringing our candidates closer to buy territory. Patience is appropriate.
